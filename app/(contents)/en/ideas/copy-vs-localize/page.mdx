# Copied Ideas vs Localized Ideas

> **In short:** Taking a model that works in Silicon Valley or India and dropping it into Bangladesh unchanged usually fails. Roads, payment habits, and how trust gets built are all different. Pathao started from the ride-sharing idea abroad but had to rebuild the model around Dhaka's roads and motorbike culture. bKash didn't build a fully digital PayPal-or-Venmo-style platform – it built an agent network that moves physical cash by hand. Chaldal moved away from a hyperlocal Indian model into its own warehouses. Before copying anything, check payment, logistics, trust, and regulation separately – all four differ here.

Seeing a foreign app or model and thinking "this would work in Bangladesh too" is natural. The trouble starts when the whole model gets lifted as-is, with only the language and logo changed. Understanding what actually changes in the Bangladeshi market lets the same underlying idea become a real business.

## Why foreign models break down here

Four places show the gap most clearly.

**Payment habits.** Western apps are built assuming a card or bank account. In 2010, more than 70 percent of Bangladesh's population lived in rural areas with virtually no access to traditional banking, and formal banking penetration sat below 15 percent (Future Startup). bKash was born directly out of that gap. Rather than building a fully digital platform like PayPal or Venmo, bKash built an agent-based network where shopkeeper-agents act as "human ATMs," converting physical cash into digital money and back again (Future Startup). By 2013 that agent network had reached 50,000 points, and grew further from there (Future Startup). Making it usable on a basic phone via USSD, with no card or smartphone required, was the actual engineering problem – one the Western model never had to solve.

**Logistics and road reality.** According to this site's own case study, Pathao started by looking at foreign ride-sharing models, but founder Hussain M Elius told Future Startup that a motorcycle-taxi culture like Vietnam's or Indonesia's simply didn't exist in Bangladesh beforehand – Pathao had to build that behavior from scratch ([Case Study: Pathao](/en/case-studies/pathao)). A car-first model dropped directly onto Dhaka's traffic and narrow lanes wouldn't have worked, so the company had to go motorbike-first.

**How trust gets built.** Western apps assume users trust a service after reading reviews or recognizing a brand. In Bangladesh, people want proof before money changes hands with a new service. Chaldal initially tried a hyperlocal model similar to India's Grofers or PepperTap – picking up items from local stores for delivery. But the lack of control over inventory left customers unhappy, so the company built its own small warehouses (5,000 to 7,000 square feet each) to take direct control (TechCrunch). Cash on delivery and an easy return policy were built in alongside this, so customers could trust the service without paying upfront first (The Business Standard).

**Regulatory reality.** Licensing rules from wherever a foreign fintech or healthtech model originates don't apply in Bangladesh. Mobile financial services fall under Bangladesh Bank's regulation; healthcare sits under a different ministry entirely. A sector that's lightly regulated in the foreign model's home market can be heavily regulated here.

## What copies over, what must be rebuilt

Lay the three models' patterns side by side and one thing becomes clear: the underlying problem being solved was borrowed from abroad (moving people and goods fast, cashless transactions, fresh grocery shopping), but the *path* to the solution had to be built locally every time.

| What copies over | What must be rebuilt |
|---|---|
| The core problem or demand (fast delivery, cashless transactions, fresh goods) | The payment mechanism (card vs. cash on delivery or agent network) |
| The core technology idea (app-based booking, matching) | The mode of delivery/transport (car vs. motorbike) |
| The business-model structure (commission, subscription) | The trust-building steps (reviews vs. going into the field and showing it in person, walking first customers through it by hand) |
| The ambition to scale | The regulatory framework and licensing |

## A checklist for validating your own idea

If a foreign model gave you an idea, ask yourself these questions before building.

- [ ] How will the customer actually pay – I've thought through whether I'm assuming a card, or whether I need cash on delivery or an agent network
- [ ] How will goods or people actually move – I've accounted for roads, traffic, and courier costs, including outside Dhaka
- [ ] How will first-time trust get built – I haven't relied on advertising alone; there's a plan to demonstrate it in person
- [ ] Whether this sector falls under a regulator (banking, MFS, health, education)
- [ ] Whether pricing reflects local income levels, rather than a direct currency conversion from the foreign price

If the answer to any of these is "I don't know," that's the first task before starting the business. A shiny foreign success story tempts founders to start designing immediately – and these are exactly the places that trip them up.

Rule of thumb: **you can borrow the problem from abroad; you cannot borrow the path to the solution. Payment, roads, trust, and regulation all have to be built from inside Bangladesh.**
