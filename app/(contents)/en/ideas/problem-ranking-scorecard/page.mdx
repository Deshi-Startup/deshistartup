# Problem Ranking Scorecard

> **In short:** The [Finding Ideas](/en/ideas/finding-ideas) page has a small, quick five-question scoring table. This page is its fuller version, for when you have several problems in hand and need to compare them side by side. Score six dimensions, weight the ones that matter more, then compare the totals. The total isn't truth – it's just a tool for comparison.

Once several problems have cleared the [Problem Validation Checklist](/en/ideas/problem-validation-checklist), the next task is deciding which one to tackle first. The checklist tells you what's real; this scorecard tells you what to do first.

## The six dimensions

Score each dimension from 0 to 5.

**1. Recency and frequency.** How recent the incident was and how often it repeats. A problem that happened last week and recurs several times a week scores 5. One you heard about once, six months ago, scores 1.

**2. Current cost.** How much money or time the problem currently costs. Clear thousands of taka or several hours a month scores higher; mere annoyance scores lower.

**3. Reach to first customers.** Whether you can name the first 10 to 50 people starting today. Naming actual people or places scores higher; saying "there are plenty of people out there" scores lower.

**4. Market breadth.** Whether the same solution can serve many more people, or needs rebuilding for each one.

**5. Competitive intensity.** Whether someone is already serving this problem well. No one working on it scores higher; two or three big players already holding the market scores lower.

**6. Personal fit and barriers.** Whether you have a skill or network advantage in this problem, and whether there's a heavy regulatory or capital barrier. More advantage and fewer barriers scores higher.

## Why and how to weight

Not every dimension matters equally. Early on, reach to first customers and current cost should usually carry the most weight, because without those two, everything else is theory. Market breadth matters more later, not necessarily in year one.

A simple weighting method: multiply reach-to-first-customers and current cost by 2. Multiply the other four dimensions by 1. Then add everything up.

## A worked example, scoring three problems side by side

Suppose Sumaiya, based in Dhaka, validated three different problems, each through ten conversations.

| Dimension (weight) | Problem 1: shop courier-order reconciliation | Problem 2: school attendance reports | Problem 3: rental-agreement drafting |
|---|---|---|---|
| Recency and frequency (×1) | 4 | 3 | 2 |
| Current cost (×2) | 5 | 3 | 2 |
| Reach to first customers (×2) | 4 | 2 | 1 |
| Market breadth (×1) | 4 | 3 | 2 |
| Competitive intensity (×1) | 3 | 4 | 4 |
| Personal fit and barriers (×1) | 4 | 2 | 2 |
| **Total (weighted)** | **33** | **22** | **16** |

The math for Problem 1: (4×1) + (5×2) + (4×2) + (4×1) + (3×1) + (4×1) = 4 + 10 + 8 + 4 + 3 + 4 = 33.

All three are hypothetical scenarios for Sumaiya, not a real company's data. Problem 1 leads mainly because its current-cost and reach scores are high, and those are the dimensions carrying more weight. Problem 3 has lower competition, but its reach and cost scores are weak, so it falls behind on the total.

## The total isn't truth – it's a tool for comparison

The scorecard doesn't prove which problem is "best." It shows you where your evidence is strong and where it's thin. When two problems score close together, the dimension with the lowest score is exactly where to focus next. Problem 2's weak "reach to first customers" score, for instance, means Sumaiya's next week should be spent talking to five more schools to actually test that number – not dropping the problem just because its total is lower.

Every score needs evidence behind it before you write it down. Scoring without evidence just means the whole scorecard rests on guesswork. The evidence-gathering step lives in the [Problem Validation Checklist](/en/ideas/problem-validation-checklist) – run that first, then score here.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've scored every dimension for every problem with evidence behind it, not a guess
- [ ] I've given reach-to-first-customers and current cost more weight
- [ ] I've compared all the problems side by side in the same table
- [ ] I haven't assumed the highest-scoring problem is automatically "best" – I've looked at which dimensions are weak
- [ ] I gathered evidence with the checklist before scoring

Rule of thumb: **the scorecard doesn't make the decision – it makes the decision visible. The real news isn't the total; it's which dimension has the least evidence.**
