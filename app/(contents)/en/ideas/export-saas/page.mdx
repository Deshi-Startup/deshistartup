# Export-Oriented SaaS from Bangladesh

> **In short:** Selling software or a service from Bangladesh to foreign customers means handling three things separately. How money legally comes back (Bangladesh Bank's export form and a fixed deadline), what tax benefit applies to the income (an ITES income tax exemption, valid until a specific date), and which government body is actually relevant (the Hi-Tech Park Authority, not BEZA or BEPZA, which serve factory-based export industries). Every one of these rules carries an expiry date, so verify the current status yourself right before you start relying on it.

If you're building software or a service aimed at foreign customers rather than the local market, the first task isn't building the product – it's understanding how the money legally comes back into the country.

## How the money actually comes back

Any export from Bangladesh, goods or services, must be declared on an "EXP" form under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947, with specific exemptions (Bangladesh Bank). This form is where the exporter undertakes to repatriate the full proceeds within a set period. For service exports (non-physical exports such as data entry, offshore IT services, or business process outsourcing), the general deadline is four months – Bangladesh Bank has instructed service exporters to repatriate earnings within this window (The Business Standard, citing a Bangladesh Bank directive). Software and IT companies don't need a separate Export Registration Certificate (ERC), but the EXP form obligation still applies. Failing to repatriate earnings on time carries the risk of punitive action under the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act.

In practice, most small software exporters use a channel like Payoneer or Wise. The critical condition is that the money must ultimately land in a Bangladeshi bank account as a traceable inward remittance – simply sitting in a Payoneer or Wise account isn't sufficient. This condition is also tied to the tax exemption (see below).

## Tax benefits on the income

Under the Sixth Schedule of the Income Tax Act 2023, income from Information Technology Enabled Services (ITES) is fully tax-exempt, provided the income arrives through a banking channel. This specific exemption is valid until 30 June 2027 (legalseba.com, citing a BASIS-published gazette document).

Two further benefits appear in public sources. Businesses established inside a Hi-Tech Park get a full tax holiday for the first 7 years, then 70 percent exemption for years 8 through 10, valid until 30 June 2035 (legalseba.com). There's also mention of a 6 percent export subsidy for software, ITES, and hardware, though this subsidy is noted as valid only until 31 December 2025 (legalseba.com). This page is being written in July 2026, which means that subsidy's deadline appears to have already passed – whether it's been renewed needs verification against NBR's current SRO (statutory regulatory order) before you rely on it.

## Which government body actually matters

Bangladesh has three export-support bodies, but each serves a different kind of business. The Bangladesh Economic Zones Authority (BEZA) and the Bangladesh Export Processing Zones Authority (BEPZA) are primarily for large-scale factories, manufacturing, and export-oriented industry that need land and physical infrastructure. For a software team running out of a home or an office, these two bodies usually aren't relevant.

The body that actually matters for software and IT-based businesses is the Bangladesh Hi-Tech Park Authority (BHTPA), which serves domestic and foreign high-tech industries, particularly ICT, digital services, software, and startups, inside hi-tech and software parks (BHTPA). Taking space inside a Hi-Tech Park is also what unlocks the tax benefits mentioned above. Alongside this, BASIS, as the software and ITES sector association, is a good source for membership, policy context, and current export-related updates.

## The practical path

For a software founder, the rough sequence looks like this.

1. Fix a channel for receiving money from foreign customers (Payoneer, Wise, or direct bank transfer), and confirm the funds ultimately land in a Bangladeshi bank account.
2. Keep the EXP form obligation and four-month deadline in mind, and confirm with your bank exactly how to declare service-export earnings.
3. Follow the banking-channel condition to claim the ITES income tax exemption, and claim it correctly on your annual tax return.
4. If your team grows or you need a fixed address, weigh the pros and cons of taking space in a Hi-Tech Park.
5. Consider BASIS membership for networking and current policy information.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've decided which channel foreign-customer money will arrive through into a Bangladeshi bank account
- [ ] I've confirmed the EXP form and four-month repatriation deadline with my bank
- [ ] I've verified the ITES tax exemption's condition (banking-channel income) and its deadline (30 June 2027, as of what's known while writing this)
- [ ] I've checked NBR's current SRO to see whether the export subsidy is still active
- [ ] I understand that BEZA/BEPZA aren't relevant to my business, and BHTPA is

Rule of thumb: **for export-oriented software, the first job isn't building the product – it's understanding how money comes back and what the tax-exemption conditions are. Both carry expiry dates, so check them year by year.**
