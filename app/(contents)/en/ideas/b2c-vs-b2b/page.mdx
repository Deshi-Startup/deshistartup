# B2C vs B2B vs B2B2C in Bangladesh

> **In short:** The real difference between the three models isn't who you're selling to – it's who decides, and when the money actually lands. In B2C, the customer decides quickly on their own, and money arrives almost immediately, though margin per order stays thin. In B2B, a shop owner or business owner decides, the sale takes longer to close, but once the relationship exists, larger and recurring orders follow. In B2B2C, you work behind another business – their customers are your real users, even though the business itself pays you. Which model to choose depends on how much capital, patience, and sales skill you have.

## What actually differs across the three models

**Sales cycle and who decides.** In B2C, an ordinary buyer decides with their own money, and can go from seeing a Facebook post to placing an order within minutes. In B2B, the decision sits with a shop owner or a person responsible for purchasing, who checks the cost, negotiates, and may take weeks to decide. In B2B2C, you have to sell directly to the business itself – the end consumer never even knows you exist.

**Cash flow.** In B2C, money arrives almost immediately, through cash on delivery or instant payment. In B2B, sales are often made on credit, with payment arriving 30 or 60 days later – you have to manage the risk of money sitting stuck in between.

**How trust gets built.** In B2C, trust comes from reviews, word of mouth, and brand recognition. In B2B, trust comes from relationships, references, and proven results – once broken, it's hard to win back.

## Real examples of the three models in Bangladesh

**B2C: Chaldal.** Chaldal sells groceries directly to consumers, delivering from its own warehouses, with cash on delivery available. A 2021 report noted the company handling 2.5 million orders a year and generating more than USD 40 million in revenue (TechCrunch). Here, each order is small, but there are many of them, and the customer decides for themselves inside the app.

**B2B: ShopUp (Mokam).** Bangladesh has roughly 4.5 million small neighborhood grocery shops, making up 98 percent of the retail sector, and these shops regularly struggle to procure goods from multiple wholesalers (Future Startup). ShopUp's Mokam platform brings the supply chain – producer to wholesaler, wholesaler to shop – into one place, adding logistics (RedX) and a supply-on-credit financing product (Baki) on top (Future Startup). Here, the customer is the shop owner – once trust is earned, large recurring orders follow, but building that first trust takes time and field staff.

**B2B2C: SSLCommerz.** SSLCommerz is a payment gateway that doesn't sell directly to the end consumer – instead, it's integrated into more than 14,000 businesses' websites and apps under contract (SSLCommerz). When an end consumer pays by card on some online shop, SSLCommerz's technology works behind the scenes, but the consumer never pays SSLCommerz directly and may not even know its name. Here, the customer (who pays) is the businesses; the user (who the service actually works for) is those businesses' own consumers.

## Which model to choose, and when

| Question | Points toward B2C | Points toward B2B |
|---|---|---|
| How much capital do you have | Can start quickly with less capital | Longer sales cycle, needs more patience and capital upfront |
| Where's your sales skill | Strong in marketing and content | Strong in relationship-building and negotiation |
| What kind of revenue suits you | Many small transactions | Fewer but larger, recurring orders |
| Can you absorb cash-flow risk | Works fine with less, money arrives fast | Need the capacity to manage credit-sale risk |

B2B2C is usually the hardest model to start with as a first business, because it requires understanding two layers of need at once – the paying business's, and their end consumer's. If you're heading toward a franchise or white-label model, this is a decision worth making upfront.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've decided who makes the decision in my business – the consumer directly, or a business owner
- [ ] I've calculated when money actually arrives – immediately, or 30-60 days later
- [ ] I've thought through how trust will be built – ads and reviews, or direct relationships
- [ ] I've checked whether my capital and patience can handle B2B's longer sales cycle
- [ ] If considering B2B2C, I've written down both layers of customer need separately

Rule of thumb: **who pays isn't the first question – who decides, and when the money lands, is the real one. Choose your model by looking at those two.**
