# How to Find Startup Ideas in Bangladesh

> **In short:** Do not begin with “Which startup should I build?” Spend seven days observing where people lose time, money, or trust. Record when each event last happened and how the person handles it now. Then interview ten people about one problem. Does the same pain repeat? Are people already spending something? Can you reach the first customers? If the answers line up, you have a problem worth a small test.

When people sit down to generate ideas, their minds jump to solutions: an app, marketplace, AI, or delivery service. Work in the opposite direction. For seven days, do not design a solution. Collect real incidents of frustration.

A useful problem has three early signs. It has actually occurred, it repeats, and people already spend time or money coping with it. “Everyone will want this later” is not a problem. “Last Tuesday I spent three hours reconciling courier payments” is one.

---

## Open a Problem Log First

Keep these fields in a notebook, notes app, or Google Sheet:

```text
Date:
Person or business type:
Job they were trying to do:
Where it became stuck:
When it last happened:
How they cope now:
Time or money lost:
Who to speak to next:
```

Do not record your assumption as an event. “Shopkeepers want to become digital” is an assumption. “Rafiq spent two hours last night reconciling credit in his paper ledger” is an event. Ideas grow from events, not slogans.

---

## 1. Look for Work Held Together by Patches

Does one job require a paper ledger, Messenger, phone calls, Google Sheets, and three different people? There may be an opportunity inside the patches. But disorder does not automatically need software. First ask how often the work happens and what a mistake costs.

Suppose Sumiya interviews five Facebook clothing sellers. Three manually compare courier-app reports with orders in a ledger. One spent two days finding which orders account for BDT 18,000 that remained unsettled last month. “An accounting app” is not yet the idea. A small first offer could be a manual reconciliation service. If the same work repeats, software may come later.

Look for situations where:

- the same information is entered in several places
- everyone depends on one person to catch mistakes
- work does not move without another call or message
- delayed reconciliation traps cash

---

## 2. Look at What People Already Pay For

Existing spending is a useful sign that a problem matters. The money may not go to software. It may go to a broker, extra employee, urgent courier, penalty, or repeated travel.

Suppose Tanvir in Bogura hires someone each week to carry papers to Dhaka. Do not ask, “Would you use an app?” Ask how many trips occurred in the last four weeks, what they cost, and what happened when papers arrived late. The amount helps define what a solution could reasonably cost.

A problem can be real without existing spending. In that case, examine time, risk, and stress. Do not set a price merely because people say the problem is “very important.”

---

## 3. Find Broken Trust and Coordination

Many activities fail not because a product is missing but because it is hard to find the right person, see work status, or rely on a promise. These situations may need a trust system before technology.

Consider home repair. The customer does not know whether a worker will arrive on time. The worker does not know whether the customer will pay. Matching two phone numbers does not solve the whole problem. Identity checks, clear pricing, work photographs, and dispute handling may also be required.

Before saying “we will build a marketplace,” write down:

- who does not trust whom
- which evidence could increase trust
- who resolves a dispute
- what that operation costs you

---

## 4. Validate Problems from Your Own Work Outside Your Workplace

Personal experience gives you a head start in understanding a problem. It does not prove that others share it.

Suppose you work at a school and spend three hours every Thursday preparing an attendance report. Ask five other schools who prepares the same report, how long it takes, which software they use, and why any existing tool fails them.

Your advantage is access to the language and first contacts. Your disadvantage is the temptation to treat your own pain as universal. Hold at least half of the conversations outside your own organisation.

---

## 5. Watch Regulatory and Technology Changes, Not Rumours

A new government rule, payment rail, or technology can open a business opportunity. Misreading a circular can close that door before the business begins.

Check three things:

1. What does the regulator's official notice actually say?
2. Whose behaviour or cost will change?
3. Is the change mandatory or merely encouraged?

Suppose a new online form launches. Before building a “form-filling app,” observe where people become stuck. The problem may not be the form. It may be collecting the required documents. That changes the solution.

---

## Score Five Problems

Give each field 0, 1, or 2. The total is not truth. It is a tool for comparing what you know.

| Question | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Have I heard a recent real event? | No | One | Several |
| How often does it happen? | Rarely | Sometimes | Regularly |
| What does it cost now? | Nothing visible | Time or frustration | Clear money or major loss |
| Where can I find the first ten people? | I do not know | Through introductions | I can reach them now |
| Can the same solution serve many more people? | No | Uncertain | Possibly |

The highest total is not automatically the best idea. The table shows which evidence is missing. A zero under recent events means more conversations. A zero under first ten people means the market may exist while your starting route is weak.

There is no rule that a problem must affect hundreds of thousands of people. A costly problem among a smaller group of businesses may support a strong market. Build from the bottom: how many people can you reach, what might they pay, and what would delivery cost?

---

## A Seven-Day Idea-Finding Sprint

- **Days 1 and 2:** record 20 incidents of frustration from work, family, markets, and businesses you know
- **Day 3:** choose the five most frequent or expensive problems
- **Days 4 to 6:** interview ten people about one problem and recent incidents
- **Day 7:** put the problem, current workaround, cost, and riskiest assumption on one page

Only then design a solution. The first test may be an app or a manually delivered service. The goal is to answer the largest unknown as cheaply as possible.

---

## Checklist

- [ ] I have created a problem log with 20 real incidents
- [ ] I have separated assumptions from reported events
- [ ] I have scored five problems with the same questions
- [ ] I have interviewed ten people about one problem
- [ ] I have recorded how they cope and what it costs
- [ ] I know where to find the first ten potential customers
- [ ] I have selected the riskiest assumption before designing a solution

Rule of thumb: **do not search for ideas. Search for incidents where people lose time, money, or trust.**
