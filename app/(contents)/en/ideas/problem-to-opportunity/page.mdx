# Turning a Problem List into a Business Opportunity

> **In short:** A problem being real doesn't automatically make it a business. Turning a problem into a business needs three questions answered. Will people pay a business for this, rather than just tolerating it themselves; can the same solution reach hundreds of people rather than needing to be rebuilt for each one; and what's left in your hands if the shopkeeper next door simply copies what you did. The same underlying problem can become a service, a piece of software, or a marketplace – each shape carries a different cost and price. Deciding the shape is the actual work here.

The [Finding Ideas](/en/ideas/finding-ideas) page walks through building a problem log and scoring five problems. It ends by saying "then think about a solution" once you've picked one from the log. This page picks up exactly where that one stops.

## A problem being real and a problem being a business are two different questions

Suppose you've talked to ten clothing-shop owners. Every one of them says that at least once a month, some money gets stuck while reconciling courier reports against orders. That's a real, proven problem. But jumping straight from there to "I'll build a reconciliation app" often lands you in the wrong place.

Becoming a business requires answering three questions that differ from the problem-validation questions.

**One, will people pay a business for this.** Tolerating a problem and paying money to remove it are not the same thing. A shopkeeper sits down and reconciles accounts for two hours himself because it's "free" – it only costs his own time. Whether he'd pay your service to do the same work is a separate question. Suppose Rahim runs five Facebook pages in Chattogram. He hasn't priced his own time, so "you'll save time" alone may not open his wallet. But "last month you lost 18,000 taka you couldn't trace to any order" changes the math – people pay to stop a direct money loss, not always just to save time.

**Two, can the same solution reach many more people.** If ten people's problems turn out to differ in shape from each other, you'll be customizing the same solution over and over – that's a service business, not an easily scalable software business. The reverse is also true: if ten people's problems fit the same mold, one solution can be sold to many.

**Three, what's left if the shopkeeper next door copies it.** Suppose you reconcile your first customer's accounts by hand and get a good result. Now ask whether the employee at the next shop could do the same thing. If the answer is yes, nothing remains except a first-mover head start. Something worth defending usually means data that compounds and makes the solution better over time, a relationship built on trust, or technology that isn't easy to copy.

## The same problem, three business shapes

The same underlying problem can become different businesses. Take the courier-order reconciliation problem and look at three shapes it could take.

**Service model.** You or one employee manually reconciles a shop's accounts every week. Suppose you charge each shop 1,500 taka a month. Handling 20 shops brings in 30,000 taka a month, with cost limited to your or an employee's time. Almost no capital is needed to start, but time scales with each new shop at the same rate.

**Software model.** You build a simple dashboard where the shop uploads its own courier report and the reconciliation happens automatically. At a 500-taka monthly subscription with 200 shops, that's 100,000 taka a month – building it costs money upfront, but each new customer afterward costs almost no additional time. The starting investment is larger, though, and shop owners need to be taught to use the system themselves.

**Marketplace or agency model.** Instead of building the solution yourself, you match several freelance bookkeepers with shops and take a commission on each transaction. Capital needed is low, but quality control is hard – if any one bookkeeper makes a mistake, the reputation damage lands on your name.

None of the three is "wrong." Which one to choose depends on how much capital and time you have, and which one your first customers are actually willing to pay for.

## Working through one decision

Suppose Nasrin runs a coaching center in Sylhet and has noticed the same problem – reconciling student attendance records takes three hours every week. In the service model, she could reconcile attendance for five nearby coaching centers herself, at 800 taka each per month – 4,000 taka in extra income, but her own time stays consumed. In the software model, building a simple attendance app and selling it to 50 coaching centers at 300 taka a month brings in 15,000 taka a month, but building the app costs money upfront, and time will go into teaching owners unfamiliar with technology how to use it. Which is better depends on how much capital and time Nasrin has – both grew from the same underlying problem.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've separated whether people already pay for this problem, or merely tolerate the time and annoyance
- [ ] I've thought through how many people the same solution can serve, versus needing to be rebuilt each time
- [ ] I've written down what's left in my hands if a neighbor copies the same approach
- [ ] I've considered the same problem as a service, software, and agency shape, all three
- [ ] I've worked out the raw numbers (cost versus price) for at least one shape

Rule of thumb: **a real problem is not automatically a business. It becomes one when someone pays, when it can reach many, and when something survives even if a neighbor copies it.**
