# Ideation and Problem Validation in the Bangladeshi Market

> **In short:** Startup ideas keep coming from the same five places. A familiar pain inside your own trade, a gap opened by new regulation or technology, adapting a foreign model, a ready-made structure like franchising, agency, or white-labeling, and a match between your own skill and what the market needs right now. Bangladesh adds one more layer on top of all five. Most transactions sit outside any paper record, ownership blurs with family businesses, and people want proof before they pay. That's why "everyone in Bangladesh will use this" claims almost always fall apart here. Whatever source your idea came from, it goes nowhere until you check it against your actual market.

Ideas rarely arrive out of nowhere. Look closely at the origin story of most startups and the same few paths keep repeating. Which path you entered through also shapes how you should validate later, so recognizing the source isn't just theory.

## What this guide covers, and what it doesn't

Want the hands-on seven-day method right away? See [How to Find Startup Ideas in Bangladesh](/en/ideas/finding-ideas). That guide walks you through collecting real incidents of frustration over seven days and interviewing ten people to land on one idea.

This page does something different. It maps out *where* ideas come from in the first place, and the realities of the Bangladeshi market that break a good-looking plan if you skip checking them.

## 1. A familiar problem inside your own trade

The most credible ideas often come from inside your own work, because you understand the problem by living it, not by being told about it.

Suppose Karim deals in poultry feed in Mymensingh. Feed prices shift almost daily, and the news travels by phone call and word of mouth, never on paper. One morning the price rises 5 taka per sack, but Karim only hears about it at noon. By then he's already sold 40 sacks at the old price. Five taka a sack means a 200-taka loss that day alone. If this happens three or four times a month, that's over a thousand taka lost, purely from hearing about the price change too late.

Karim understands this pain clearly because he's lived it. But an old piece of backlog advice applies here: match your own trade's expertise against the idea. If Karim talks to ten other dealers in his area, he'll likely find this delay problem isn't his alone – it belongs to the whole dealer network. A problem from inside your own trade is a good starting point for an idea, but it isn't proof. The validation tools for that live in [How to Find Startup Ideas in Bangladesh](/en/ideas/finding-ideas).

## 2. A gap opened by new regulation or technology

When the government introduces a new rule, or a technology suddenly becomes more accessible, a new gap opens in the market. BIDA's investment guidance and LightCastle Partners' market reports repeatedly list the same handful of sectors as high-growth: fintech, agritech, edtech, healthtech, logistics, and e-commerce (LightCastle Partners, BIDA). Much of that growth traces back to one common cause – mobile financial services, internet access, and government policy making something newly possible together.

But jumping into a business simply because "the sector is growing" is a good way to get burned. Suppose the government announces that a certain return for small businesses must now be filed online. Three things need separating here: what the notice actually says, whose cost or time it changes, and whether the change is mandatory or merely encouraged. Often the real friction isn't the new form itself but gathering the documents the form requires. How fast AI-driven customer service is actually growing in Bangladesh still needs verification with a reliable number, so don't set a price or market size off sector-level hype alone. Sector-by-sector data is not this guide's job – see [Common Opportunity Sectors in Bangladesh](/en/ideas/opportunity-sectors).

## 3. Bringing in a foreign model, adapted rather than copied

Something that works abroad often breaks when dropped into Bangladesh unchanged. Roads, income levels, payment habits, and how trust gets built are all different. This site's own case study shows this well – Pathao started by looking at foreign ride-sharing models but had to adapt the model to Dhaka's roads, traffic, and motorbike-first reality ([Case Study: Pathao](/en/case-studies/pathao)).

Deciding what to copy and what must change is its own separate task. We're only naming it here – the detailed method lives in [Copied Ideas vs Localized Ideas](/en/ideas/copy-vs-localize).

## 4. Franchise, agency, and white-label models

You can find an idea without inventing anything new, by borrowing a structure someone else already built. A franchise means running a business under an established brand's rules and support, in exchange for a fee or a share of profit. White-labeling means you build the product or service, but it sells under another company's name and branding.

Suppose Sumaiya first tried building inventory software under her own name. Sales weren't coming – she had to convince every shop, from scratch, why the software was worth it. Later the team changed approach: the same code now sells under three other companies' names and branding – that's the white-label model. Each client pays 80,000 taka a month under contract; three clients together bring in 240,000 taka a month. After server, maintenance, and one support staffer's cost, roughly a lakh remains, without the trouble of building her own brand.

With a franchise or white-label model, the business-model decision has to come first – will you go directly to the customer, or work behind another business? The decision tool for that lives in [B2C vs B2B in Bangladesh](/en/ideas/b2c-vs-b2b). If your skill is in software or service and you want to reach foreign customers rather than the local market, see [Export-Oriented SaaS from Bangladesh](/en/ideas/export-saas).

## 5. Skill-market fit

Having a skill doesn't automatically make a business – the market has to need that exact skill at that exact moment. Call this skill-market fit: your hands-on skill and the market's current need meeting at one point. A blacksmith's hands stay equally skilled year-round, but demand for sickles rises at harvest and falls in winter. In the same way, a person's skill can sit ready while the market simply isn't looking for it yet.

Suppose Nasrin has taught English and math in Sylhet for years, her results with students proving the skill. After the pandemic, many parents started looking for an online alternative to sending their child to in-person coaching, because of distance or time. Nasrin's skill suddenly matched a new demand. Taking 30 students into a monthly online batch at 800 taka each brings in 24,000 taka a month, with cost limited to internet and time. The idea here isn't inventing something new – it's placing an old skill where a new demand appeared.

List your own skills and set them beside the market's shifting needs. Wherever they overlap is where your first idea lives.

## The Bangladeshi reality: why "everyone will use this" doesn't hold up here

Whichever source an idea comes from, three realities in the Bangladeshi market change the validation math.

First, most small businesses run inside the informal economy – meaning business happens without digital transaction records, VAT invoices, or registration. A large part of the market simply isn't counted anywhere, not in official statistics, not in your own survey either. "There are lakhs of shops, and if each pays just 100 taka a month that's a crore" looks great on paper and is nearly impossible to capture in practice.

Second, in many businesses the person who decides isn't the person who does the work. Suppose a son at a family grocery shop wants an app to track accounts, but his father, who owns the shop, decides whether to spend money or adopt a new rule. However good the app is, it won't sell if the father isn't convinced. In family businesses, ownership and usage are often different people – recognizing that gap early matters.

Third, this is a low-trust market. People are reluctant to pay upfront, which is exactly why cash on delivery is so popular, and trust in a new service tends to travel by word of mouth rather than advertising. Put these three together and you see why "everyone will use this" collapses – founder interviews published on Future Startup repeat the same pattern: first-year customers came from a small, known circle, not from some vast market (Future Startup).

So build the math from the bottom up. Suppose an idea's potential market looks like 500,000 shops. In year one, after earning trust, you might convince 500 shops, and only if you spend the time to visit each one directly. Five hundred shops paying 100 taka a month comes to 50,000 taka a month – a long way from the crore-taka dream. Planning with that gap already accepted leads to less disappointment and more realistic decisions.

## Checklist

- [ ] I've written down which of the five sources my idea came from
- [ ] If it's a problem from my own trade, I've checked it against people outside my own circle too
- [ ] If it's a regulatory or technology gap, I read the actual notice myself, not just the sector-level hype
- [ ] If it's a foreign model, I've separated which parts need adapting
- [ ] If it's franchise or white-label, I've decided whether I'll run my own brand or work for someone else's
- [ ] I've thought through all three: how informal the market is, who actually decides, and how trust gets earned
- [ ] I've built the market-size estimate from the bottom up, not assumed it from the top down

Rule of thumb: **where the idea came from is the first question; whether everyone will adopt it is the last. Everything in between is validation.**
