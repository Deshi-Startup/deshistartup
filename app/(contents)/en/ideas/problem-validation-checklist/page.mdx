# Problem Validation Checklist

> **In short:** Before committing to a problem, check six kinds of evidence. Whether the problem has actually happened, whether people are already spending something on it, whether you can reach the first customers, whether someone has already solved it, whether the sector is regulated, and whether you have any pricing signal. This isn't a scoring table – it's a checklist for gathering evidence, to see what passes and what's still a gap. Reopen this same page for every new idea.

The [Finding Ideas](/en/ideas/finding-ideas) page talks about building a problem log. Once a problem from that log catches your eye, run it through this checklist before pouring time into it. This isn't a one-time exercise – fill this page out separately for every problem that comes to mind, ideally copied into your own notes or a Google Sheet.

There are six groups below. Each has a few lines, each line one piece of evidence. If you can't check a box, that's fine – write that line into your to-do list instead. Which group ends up with nothing checked is itself the real signal.

There's no scoring here. If you have several problems on hand and want to compare them, the [Problem Ranking Scorecard](/en/ideas/problem-ranking-scorecard) page has the scoring table for that. This page's job is only to gather evidence, not to pick a winner.

---

## 1. The problem actually happened – not an assumption

- [ ] I've heard the same kind of incident from at least 3 different people or businesses, each with a matching date
- [ ] Every incident has "when it last happened" written next to it. Last week means the problem is still alive; six months ago might mean it resolved itself
- [ ] I haven't recorded a "probably" or "I think" guess as if it were a real problem

Suppose you think, "shopkeepers find bookkeeping hard." That's an assumption. "Rafiq spent two hours last night reconciling credit in his paper ledger" is an actual incident. Nothing gets built from the first one.

## 2. People are already spending time or money on it

- [ ] I've written down whether anyone currently pays money for this problem. It shows up through a broker, an extra hire, a penalty, or an urgent courier too, not only through software
- [ ] I've put a number on how much money or how many hours go into it per week or month, even a rough one
- [ ] Even without money changing hands, I've written down how much time, risk, or stress it costs. I haven't stopped at just hearing "it's very important"

## 3. I can reach the first customers

- [ ] I can name where I'd find the first 10 people starting today – a Facebook group, a market, a known network
- [ ] I've noted what I'd need to reach them: an introduction, a reference, or a budgeted boost
- [ ] If the market looks large but the first 10 people are still strangers to me, I've flagged that separately

For a broad picture of how many people are active on Facebook or the internet in Bangladesh, see [DataReportal's Bangladesh report](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2026-bangladesh). It's not a guarantee of reaching your first 10, only context.

## 4. Whether someone has already solved it

- [ ] I've spent at least 15 minutes searching Google and Facebook, in both Bangla and English
- [ ] If I found an app, page, or service already working on the same problem in Bangladesh, I've written its name down
- [ ] If I found a competitor, I've written down why people aren't fully using them yet – price, trust, reach, or missing features

If it's a software or IT-driven solution, glance through [BASIS](https://basis.org.bd)'s member list. For the bigger market picture, [LightCastle Partners](https://lightcastlepartners.com/insights)'s reports can also show whether someone is already working in this space.

## 5. Whether the sector is regulated

- [ ] I've checked whether the problem touches banking, MFS (mobile financial services, like bKash or Nagad), health, or education
- [ ] If it does, I've written down what license or approval might be needed. If I'm not sure, I've written "needs verification" instead of guessing
- [ ] I've roughly thought through how much the licensing hassle would add to startup time and cost

Suppose the idea is an app giving small loans to rural people. Banking-style regulation applies here – you can't proceed without Bangladesh Bank's approval. It's better to check this in the idea's first week, not after six months of work.

## 6. I've found some pricing signal

- [ ] I've written down what people currently pay for a nearby solution, if one exists
- [ ] I've thought through whether my planned solution's price can sit near that number
- [ ] I've found at least 1 person who, on hearing the price, said "when can we start" rather than just "let me think about it"

---

## What the whole picture is telling you

If nothing checked in a group, that doesn't mean rejecting the problem outright – it means more work remains in that group. An empty group 1 or 2 means the problem isn't yet an incident, just a notion. An empty group 3 means you need to find a route to the first customers before building the solution. Heavy regulation in group 5, with no legal help on hand, means keeping both time and cost in mind before moving forward.

Once several problems clear this checklist, the next step is scoring them with the [Problem Ranking Scorecard](/en/ideas/problem-ranking-scorecard) to pick one. The checklist tells you what's real; the scorecard tells you what to tackle first.

Rule of thumb: **a problem without evidence is just a notion. Before building anything, ask for at least one real proof in each of the six groups.**
