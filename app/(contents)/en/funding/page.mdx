# Funding Roadmap – Where Startup Money Actually Comes From in Bangladesh

> **Summary:** Funding does not mean VC. Savings and early revenue are often the first options available to a founder. Grants, angels, VC and bank loans each have different terms. Write down why the money is needed, then match the source to the company's stage.

Say your first product version is live and orders are trickling in. The thought creeps in – "without funding, we can't grow." Stop and flip the question: what exactly would the money be spent on? If the answer isn't crisp, funding won't solve your problem – it will become a new one.

Funding is fuel, not an engine. The engine is a model where customers come, pay, and come back. Pour fuel on a business without an engine and you only get a bigger fire. So before hunting investors, finish the [idea validation](/en/validation) and [finding customers](/en/customers) work.

---

## The sources at a glance

Startup money in Bangladesh arrives through roughly seven doors. Some take equity, some want repayment with interest, and others impose spending and reporting conditions.

Equity means a share of the company's ownership. Anyone who invests for equity becomes your co-owner – of the profits and of the decisions.

| Source | What it gives, what it takes | When it fits |
|---|---|---|
| Your own money (bootstrapping) | Savings plus business revenue. Takes nothing | Almost everyone starts here |
| Family & friends | Trust money, often on unwritten terms – which is the danger | Small amounts, written terms |
| Grants & competitions | Usually no repayment or equity, but spending and reporting conditions apply | Idea and MVP stage |
| Accelerators | Training, mentors, network and sometimes cash; equity and other terms vary | Product exists, you need structured support |
| Angel investors | Individuals investing their own money, for equity | After first traction |
| VC (venture capital) | Fund money, larger amounts. Takes equity and expects fast growth | Model proven, time to scale |
| Bank / SME loans | Repayment with interest, may need collateral or a guarantor | Steady revenue, reliable cash flow |

Using your own money requires no application, but grants, debt and equity each require different evidence. Do not read the table as easiest to hardest; compare the terms with your stage.

## Think stage first

Where you stand today decides which door is worth knocking on.

| Your situation | The realistic path |
|---|---|
| Idea, no product | Own money, small tests, relevant grants or competitions |
| MVP live, a few users | Grants, accelerators, family & friends (written terms) |
| Regular sales, customers returning | Angels, a seed round, government funds |
| Proven model, ready to scale | VC, larger angel rounds, strategic investors |
| Profitable, no rush to scale | Revenue plus a bank/SME loan – why sell equity at all? |

At the idea stage, ask what evidence you could show before approaching a VC. For a proven model, compare the cost of debt with the cost of equity. Stage first, source second.

## Grants and government programs

Grants generally require neither repayment nor equity, but they do come with rules on spending, delivery and reporting. Places to watch in Bangladesh include:

- **iDEA Project** – pre-seed grants and training for startups, under the ICT Division.
- **Bangladesh Bank startup financing programme** – a framework for participating banks and financial institutions to lend to eligible startups.
- **Startup Bangladesh Limited** – the state-backed venture capital company, invests for equity.
- **Competitions** – watch announcements from universities, banks, telecom companies and donor organisations. Read each award, eligibility rule and condition separately.

Amounts, eligibility and deadlines change frequently – don't memorize numbers from anywhere (including here); check the portal before applying.

One warning: grant-chasing can become a habit. Winning competition after competition while the customer count stays flat means the real work has stalled.

## What investors actually look at

Investors aren't buying your slide design – they're buying evidence. Before any meeting, ask yourself:

- **Traction:** how many paying customers? Do they come back?
- **Market:** how many people have this problem, and what do they spend on it today?
- **Team:** why are you the right people for this particular problem?
- **Numbers:** monthly in, monthly out, months of runway left – can you say it in one minute?
- **Plan:** exactly where would the money go, and what would it speed up?

If the answers are unclear, organise the evidence and numbers first. The [30-day roadmap](/en/start-here/30-day-roadmap) helps.

## Understand these before selling equity

Giving equity creates another party's ownership rights. That stake does not return by itself without a later transfer or buyback process. Before signing, make sure you understand:

- **Valuation** – the price of the company, which sets how much ownership a given amount of money buys.
- **Dilution** – issuing new shares reduces existing owners' percentages. Model the result before a new-share round.
- **Term sheet** – the draft of the key investment terms. The non-money clauses matter most: board seats, veto rights, rights in the next round.
- **Cap table** – the list of who owns what percentage. Put family money here if it bought equity; record a loan separately as debt.

Before signing a significant deal, have a lawyer experienced in startup investment review the term sheet. A chartered accountant can review the tax and accounting effects.

And remember: taking equity investment generally requires a private limited company. See [company types](/en/company-types) and the [company registration journey](/en/journeys/register-a-private-limited-company) to get the structure right.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---|---|
| Hunting investors before proof | Paying customers first, pitch second |
| Family money on unwritten terms | Amount, terms, repayment plan – one page, two signatures |
| Giving up equity without understanding valuation | A big slice sold cheap early makes the next round hard |
| Treating funding as success | Funding means more responsibility. Celebrate customer revenue instead |
| Betting months on one investor | Talk to several in parallel; nothing is real until it's in writing |
| Mixing personal and business money | Separate the records from day one or the transactions will be difficult to explain in due diligence |

---

## Checklist before you look for funding

- [ ] Written one page on exactly what the money is for
- [ ] Have proof: paying customers or hard traction
- [ ] Monthly income/expense and runway numbers at hand
- [ ] Cap table is clean – family money included
- [ ] Checked whether the company structure can take equity investment
- [ ] Picked the source (grant/angel/VC/loan) based on stage
- [ ] Understand the key term-sheet words, and know who will review the deal
