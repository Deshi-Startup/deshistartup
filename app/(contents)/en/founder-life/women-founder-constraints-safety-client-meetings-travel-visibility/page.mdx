# The Woman Founder's Reality – Safety, Client Meetings, Travel and Visibility

> **Summary:** Building a business as a woman in Bangladesh comes with extra obstacles – family suspicion, safety calculations, inbox harassment, and the "running a page is fine, but why a business?" attitude. These are features of the environment, not your failure. This page gives working rules for handling them – where to hold meetings, what to do when harassment starts, and how to keep ownership of the business in your own hands.

Imagine Sumaiya, who runs a homemade-food page from Chattogram. Orders are growing, and a corporate office has just proposed a regular lunch contract. Her first question isn't about the business – it's "Do I go to the meeting alone? What do I tell my family? Can this person be trusted?" These questions rarely get discussed openly, yet every woman founder answers them daily.

This page is for all of them – students, employees, homemakers, mothers – anyone building something of her own. Whether the business is a Facebook page or a software startup, the obstacles are familiar. And they can be managed.

---

## Getting the family on board

For many, the first obstacle isn't the market – it's home. "Your studies will suffer," "What happens after marriage," "Why should a girl run around alone" – arguments rarely win against these. Evidence does.

- Don't start with a big announcement. Start small and keep the numbers for your first 20–30 orders in a notebook or a sheet.
- Show the month-end figures – what came in, what the profit was. Families don't trust stories; they trust accounts.
- Pull one family member into a small role. Mother on packing, brother on delivery tracking – whoever gets involved stops being the opposition.
- Set a clear limit: "I'll give this six months, and I won't put in more than this amount." Boundaries build trust.

More on relationships and pressure in the [Founder Life](/en/founder-life) section.

## Safety rules for client meetings

Skipping meetings is not the solution – having rules is. Set these for yourself and allow no exceptions:

- **Online first, in person later.** Do the first conversation on a video call. Most business gets settled right there.
- **You pick the venue.** Daytime, a familiar café, restaurant or co-working space. Never someone's home or an empty office for a first meeting.
- **Tell someone.** Where, with whom, for how long – let family or a friend know in advance, and keep phone location sharing on.
- **Bringing someone is not weakness.** Taking a colleague or partner to a big-deal meeting looks more professional, not less.
- **Leave the moment it feels wrong.** No explanation needed. "I have to go – let's settle the rest over messages" is enough. Your safety is always worth more than any single deal.

## Travel and delivery

Plenty of women hear the "all this running around, as a girl?" line. Instead of arguing, engineer the running around out of the business:

- Drop the habit of delivering yourself early. Couriers like Pathao, Steadfast and RedX pick parcels up from your home – use the pickup service.
- Handle distant suppliers by phone, WhatsApp and mobile banking as much as possible. If you must visit a market, go in daylight, with someone you know if you can.
- When using ride-sharing, share trip details with a family member.
- Operating from outside Dhaka? Don't let it discourage you – that's the great advantage of an online business. Customers don't see your location; they see your product and how you respond.

## Being visible online – how much, and how

Build the brand around your own name and photo, or stay behind the page's name – both work. Plenty of successful page owners have never been seen by their customers. Whichever gives you and your family peace of mind is the right path. A few things to keep in mind:

- Trust can be built without showing your face – real product photos, customer reviews, fast replies, a clear return policy. The [finding customers](/en/customers) guide covers these in detail.
- Keep personal and business numbers separate. Get a separate SIM for the business – inbox, calls and WhatsApp all live there.
- Don't reply to harassing messages. Screenshot, block, report to Facebook. Saved evidence becomes useful later.
- If it escalates to threats or persistent harassment, don't suffer in silence. The police run a cyber support unit that takes complaints about online harassment of women – tell someone in your family and file the complaint.
- When haggling in the inbox turns into needless personal questions, pull the conversation back to business: "Price and delivery details are above. Would you like to order?"

## Keep the business in your own name

This is the most important and least discussed point. For household convenience, many women run the trade license in the husband's name, bKash on a brother's number, the bank account in the father's name. Three years later it turns out the business, on paper, isn't yours.

- Get the [trade license](/en/trade-license) in your own name. There is no extra barrier to doing this as a woman.
- Get your [e-TIN](/en/e-tin-vat-bin) in your own name – it's done online.
- Receive business money into a bank account or merchant wallet in your own name. Details in the [payments guide](/en/payments).
- If you partner with anyone – even your own brother – write the shares and responsibilities on one page and both sign it.

Later, when you want funding, apply for a loan, or sell the business, these papers are what prove whose business it is. Ownership that slips into someone else's name is hard – sometimes impossible – to get back. For the funding paths themselves, see the [funding roadmap](/en/funding-roadmap).

## You don't have to fight alone

The women's entrepreneur community in Bangladesh is now enormous. In Facebook communities like WE (Women and E-commerce), hundreds of thousands of women sellers answer each other's questions – which courier is reliable, which supplier cheats, what to do when a buyer refuses to pay. Women founders are also active in general founder groups like BD Startup Founders.

The community gives you something more valuable than answers – the reassurance that "plenty of women like me are making this work." Thousands have walked this road before you.

## Checklist

- [ ] Keeping income/expense records from the first months, in a form the family can see
- [ ] Written my own client-meeting rules (venue, time, who to inform)
- [ ] Set up a separate business number and inbox
- [ ] Decided what I'll do if harassment starts (screenshot → block → report)
- [ ] Planned to get the trade license, TIN and payments in my own name
- [ ] Joined at least one women-entrepreneur community
- [ ] Using courier pickup – not delivering myself
