# Idea Validation

> **TL;DR:** Validation is not asking, “Do you like my idea?” It is learning whether the problem occurs, how people handle it now, what it costs them, and whether they will commit time or money to a better option. Building first and looking for customers later is a common and expensive mistake.

A plain operational problem can become a stronger business than an impressive-sounding idea. What matters is the depth and frequency of the problem, the current alternative and evidence that someone will act.

## Where Ideas Come From

Useful ideas often start in one of three places: your own experience, a problem observed in someone else's work, or a pattern in market data.

Personal experience gives context, but you are not the entire market. An idea based on other people's work demands careful listening. Data can show direction, but customer conversations reveal language, hesitation, budget and the real decision process.

## Step 1: Write the Problem in One Line

Do not begin with “app”, “platform”, “AI” or “marketplace”. Write:

```text
We help [specific people] with [a specific problem],
which they currently handle through [the present alternative].
```

A useful problem statement names the person, problem and present alternative. “Make online shopping easier” is vague. “Help clothing sellers outside Dhaka reconcile cash-on-delivery returns” is testable.

Ask:

- When did the problem last happen?
- What do they do now?
- What time, money or trust does it cost?
- Who decides and who pays?
- What happens if it is left unsolved?

## Step 2: Interview Potential Customers

Use twenty to thirty conversations as a possible starting target, not a law. The goal is to hear recurring patterns while including people beyond friends and family.

| Ask | Avoid |
|---|---|
| “When did this last happen?” | “Would you buy this?” |
| “How do you handle it now?” | “Do you like my idea?” |
| “What does it cost in time or money?” | “How much would you pay?” |
| “What have you tried?” | “Do you need this feature?” |
| “Whose approval is required?” | “Would everyone use it?” |

Do not pitch at the start of the interview. At the end, you can ask permission to return with a small test.

## Step 3: Map Alternatives and Competitors

A competitor shows that someone else has noticed the customer or task, but it is not proof that the market works. Look for customers, usage and revenue where evidence is public.

The real alternative may be a Facebook page, WhatsApp group, neighbourhood shop, broker, known supplier or handwritten ledger. Record why the customer uses it, what they dislike, the switching cost and where you might be meaningfully better.

## Step 4: Run the Smallest Useful Test

An MVP is the smallest test of the riskiest assumption, not a poorly built version of the whole product.

| Idea | Small test |
|---|---|
| Food delivery | Take and deliver twenty orders through a page and phone |
| Shop-accounting SaaS | Prepare weekly reports manually for five shops |
| Online tutoring | Run one live class for a small batch |
| Product e-commerce | Post ten items and track orders, returns and margin |
| B2B marketplace | Manually match buyers with suppliers |

Afterward, record views, conversations, price questions, payments, refusals, repeated objections and repeat use.

## Step 5: Look for Commitment

Praise is easy; giving money or time is harder. Stronger evidence includes an advance, paid pilot, repeat manual order, written pilot approval, a scheduled demo, shared operating data or an introduction to the real decision-maker.

“Tell me when it launches” and “I would try it if it were free” are weak signals. Record them, but do not build a decision on them.

## Step 6: Size a Reachable Market

The country's total population is not your market. Your market is the people or organisations that have the problem, whom you can reach, and who can pay.

| Level | Question |
|---|---|
| Broad market | How many people or organisations may face the problem? |
| Reachable market | Whom can you realistically reach in the next one or two years? |
| First market | Who might be the first fifty or hundred customers? |

Check whether location, language, trust, delivery, payments, family decisions, Eid or other seasonal patterns change the behaviour.

## Validation Notes

| Field | Record |
|---|---|
| Customer | Type and location |
| Problem | Last occurrence and context |
| Current alternative | What they use today |
| Cost | Time, money, friction or trust |
| Decision | Who decides and who pays |
| Evidence | Payment, pilot, commitment or objection |
| Next step | Follow-up, demo or price test |
