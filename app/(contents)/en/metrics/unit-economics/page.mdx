# Unit Economics: What One Order Actually Leaves You

> **In short:** Unit economics means the maths of a single order or a single customer, deducting every cost to see what's actually left at the end. Most sellers judge profit from "buying price versus selling price" alone, but once you count courier, packaging, ads, and returned orders, the picture often flips. If one order doesn't pay, selling more only deepens the loss. So before you scale, today's one job is this: put the real costs of your last 30 orders in and find out what one order truly leaves you.

Imagine Rahim, running a Facebook clothing page in Chittagong. A shirt he bought for 800 taka sells for 1,200. That's 400 taka profit, right? Believing in that 400, Rahim decides the business is profitable and pours more into boosting. Yet at month's end nothing has built up in the bank; if anything it has shrunk. Where did the maths cheat him?

Here's where: Rahim only subtracted the shirt's cost. But several other costs quietly took their cut before that one order reached his hand. Unit economics is exactly the tool that drags those hidden costs into the light.

## What unit economics actually is

A "unit" is the smallest sales event in your business, the one that repeats. For Rahim it's one order; for a coaching centre, one enrolment; for a subscription app, one customer. Unit economics is the profit-and-loss maths of that single unit.

Its core number is called **contribution margin**, meaning what's left from one unit's sale after deducting every cost directly tied to that unit. Think of the credit ledger at a shop. You count the till at day's end, but until the credit comes in and the costs behind those sales are taken out, the real profit is unknown. Contribution margin hands you that real figure, per unit.

For you this means: a signboard or a month's total sales won't tell you whether the business is profitable. What one order really leaves you will.

## The full maths of one order

Let's break Rahim's 1,200-taka shirt order down step by step. At each step, watch what's left (the numbers are illustrative; put your own rates in):

| Step | Taka | Left in hand |
|---|---|---|
| Sale (one order) | 1,200 | 1,200 |
| Less shirt cost (COGS) | 800 | 400 |
| Less courier | 120 | 280 |
| Less packaging | 20 | 260 |
| Less courier cash-collection fee | 20 | 240 |
| Less ad cost to win the order | 80 | 160 |
| **Contribution margin per order** | | **160** |

So not 400 but 160 taka is what Rahim actually keeps. Where did that 80 taka of ads come from? Say Rahim boosts with 10,000 taka a month and gets 125 orders. That's an average ad cost of 80 taka to win one order, and that is exactly **CAC (customer acquisition cost)**, the cost of winning one customer. The plain meaning of the term is in the [glossary](/en/start-here/glossary).

### Now take the hit from returned orders

The biggest leak is still ahead. Not every cash-on-delivery (COD) parcel gets accepted. On a returned one the sale is zero, yet the two-way courier, the packaging, and the ad spend that won it all remain. This return is called **RTO (return to origin)**, and it's where COD businesses lose the most money. Ways to manage it are in the [Payments](/en/payments) section.

Say 1 in every 5 of Rahim's orders comes back. The loss on one returned order is 200 taka of two-way courier, 20 of packaging, 80 of ads, so 300 taka in all. Now take 5 orders together. 4 go through fine, leaving 160 each, so 640 taka. 1 comes back and eats 300. Real contribution across the 5 orders is 640 minus 300, that is 340 taka. Divide by orders and it's 68 taka each.

See what happened: 160 on the ledger, 68 in reality once returns are counted. This one number saves Rahim, because now it's clear whether his shop rent, his own pocket money, and his phone bill will come out of that 68 taka. Push the return rate from 1-in-5 to 1-in-3 and the maths goes into a loss, something he'd otherwise only notice by staring at the bank at month's end.

## One order or one customer, which to count

For a sell-once business like Rahim's, counting one order as the unit is fine. But if a customer buys again and again, the per-order figure hides the real picture, because CAC is paid only once while a customer who buys three times gives contribution three times.

Say a customer pays Sumaiya's software 500 taka a month, leaving her 100 taka of contribution. Winning that customer cost 500 taka of CAC. So that cost takes 5 months to come back, which is called **CAC payback**, the time for the cost to return. If the customer stays 10 months on average, the remaining 5 are pure profit. So in subscription or buy-again businesses, count one customer as the unit and match CAC against the profit across the whole relationship, that is LTV. The deeper maths of LTV and CAC is in the [Metrics](/en/metrics) section.

The rule of thumb here is simple: sell once, count one order; sell repeatedly, count one customer.

## When to call the business profitable

Clear three gates and you know the unit maths is healthy:

- **Contribution is positive.** Something is left after every direct cost on one unit. If nothing is, the more you sell the deeper you sink; selling more is then the danger, not the fix.
- **Contribution covers fixed costs.** The month's fixed costs like rent, salary, and your own pocket money get covered by the contribution of however many orders; that point is break-even. The method is in [the one-page plan](/en/start-here/one-page-startup-plan).
- **Per-customer profit far exceeds CAC.** In buy-again businesses, the profit from one customer is generally considered healthy at three times CAC or more, and the sooner CAC comes back, the safer you are.

## Where the maths goes wrong

- **Subtracting only the buying price.** Rahim's whole 400-versus-160 gap is this mistake. Courier, packaging, fees, ads, all of it is a cost of that one order.
- **Not counting returned orders at all.** Leave returns out and every number looks prettier than it is. The more COD, the bigger this gap.
- **Blending old customers' cheap maths into new customers' cost.** Old customers return for free, so average CAC looks low, but winning the next 100 orders needs fresh boosting. Make the decision to scale on the real cost of a new order.
- **Costing your own labour at zero.** Grind day and night without booking a salary and the maths shows false profit. Put a price on your own time.
- **Trusting that "scale will fix it."** With negative contribution, scaling means dying faster. Make one order pay first, then grow.

## Build your own sheet

You don't need big software; one Google Sheet is enough. What you need is the real numbers from your last 30 orders. Put them in these columns:

| Column | What goes in |
|---|---|
| Sale | The order's total taka |
| Buying price (COGS) | Cost to buy or make that item |
| Courier + fee | Delivery and cash-collection cost |
| Packaging | Box, tape, poly |
| Ad per order | Month's boost divided by month's orders |
| Contribution | Sale minus every other column |

Add up the contribution across all orders and average it over the number of returns, and out comes your real per-order profit. Zero cost, about an hour. You'll know the job is done when you can say in one sentence: "One of my orders, counting returns, actually leaves me ___ taka."

## Checklist

- Wrote down sale, buying price, courier, packaging, and fee for the last 30 orders
- Divided the month's boost by orders and entered a per-order CAC
- Counted the loss on returned orders separately
- Worked out real per-order contribution with returns folded in
- Know whether this contribution covers the month's rent, salary, and pocket money
- For a buy-again business, worked the per-customer maths once too

Rule of thumb: settle the maths of one order before you scale. If one order doesn't pay, a thousand orders lose a thousand times as much.
