# Legal Roadmap: From Idea to Formal Business

> **TL;DR:** Legal setup is not a one-day checklist. The right sequence depends on your stage, risk, customers, co-founders, funding plans and sector. Use this page to decide what comes next, then confirm current requirements with the relevant authority or a qualified adviser.

Founders often make one of two mistakes. Some incorporate before testing the idea and inherit cost and filing work too early. Others take regular payments, hire people and serve corporate customers without written terms or clean records. Both create avoidable risk.

## Start with Your Stage

| Stage | What matters now | What to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Research | Customer conversations, problem testing, notes | Incorporating on the strength of an idea alone |
| Small test | Payment, refunds, liability and data | Large verbal promises |
| Regular sales | Trade license, TIN, banking, payments and invoices | Mixing personal and business money |
| Co-founders or team | Roles, ownership, IP and written terms | Assuming friendship replaces an agreement |
| Investment or corporate sales | Company structure, accounts and contracts | A confused cap table or undocumented family money |
| Regulated sector | Licenses, approvals and safety rules | Launching first and checking later |

Food, healthcare, fintech, lending, transport, import-export, telecom, data-heavy products and manufacturing may require more than a trade license.

## The Usual Sequence

1. **Choose the structure.** Compare a sole proprietorship, partnership, private limited company or other applicable form.
2. **Check the name.** Consider the brand, domain, trademark risk and RJSC clearance together.
3. **Complete applicable registration.** Companies and registered partnerships involve RJSC. A sole proprietorship has no separate incorporation, but it may still need a trade license, tax records and sector permits.
4. **Establish the tax identity.** Separate personal and company TINs where applicable and understand return obligations.
5. **Obtain the trade license.** Start with the authority responsible for the business address.
6. **Separate banking and payments.** Keep business receipts, settlements and personal spending apart.
7. **Decide whether VAT registration or enlistment applies.** Check current NBR thresholds and any rules for your category.
8. **Write the terms.** Co-founder, supplier, freelancer, employee, customer, refund and privacy terms may all matter.
9. **Build a compliance calendar.** Track renewals, returns, RJSC filings, audits and contract reviews.

## Structure at a Glance

| Issue | Sole proprietorship | Partnership | Private limited company |
|---|---|---|---|
| Setup | Relatively simple | Moderate | More documents |
| Liability | Higher personal exposure | Partners generally have unlimited personal liability | Liability limited by shares |
| Equity investment readiness | Low | Low to moderate | Higher |
| Recurring filings | Relatively light | Moderate | Heavier |
| Common fit | Solo services or a small shop | Several people trading or providing services | A scalable startup, B2B work, funding or a larger team |

This is not legal advice. Read [Company Types](company-types) and obtain professional advice where needed.

## Keep Names and Addresses Consistent

Small differences in a name or address can delay bank, VAT, payment or renewal checks. Keep the following ready and consistent:

| Document or information | Where it may be used |
|---|---|
| National ID, photograph, phone and email | Owner, director or shareholder checks |
| Business address, tenancy agreement and utility bill | Trade license, VAT and banking |
| Business name and activity | Trade license, RJSC and VAT |
| Incorporation certificate, MoA/AoA and “Form XII” | Private limited company checks |
| Board resolution | Banking, payment and licensing |
| TIN certificate | Tax, banking and VAT |
| Accounts and bank statements | Payment providers, investors and lenders |

## When Written Agreements Matter

An agreement does not have to be fifty pages. A short, clear record is better than relying on memory.

| Situation | What to record |
|---|---|
| Co-founders | Roles, equity, time, decisions and exit rules |
| Freelancer or developer | Deliverables, payment, IP and confidentiality |
| Supplier | Price, quality, delivery, returns and credit terms |
| Corporate pilot | Scope, timing, payment, data and termination |
| Employee or intern | Pay, probation, duties and confidentiality |
| Online customers | Terms, refunds, returns, privacy and delivery timing |

## Build a Compliance Calendar

A simple sheet is enough. Record the task, owner, source, last completion date and next deadline for trade-license renewal, tax returns, VAT returns, RJSC filings, audits and contract reviews.

If a deadline has already passed, do not invent or backdate records. Ask the relevant professional how to correct the position lawfully.
