# Personal e-TIN vs Company TIN: Which One to Use

> **Key Takeaway:** A personal e-TIN identifies you for your own tax affairs. A company TIN identifies the incorporated company. When a client buys from the company, keep the invoice, revenue, and expense records in the company's name. When the company pays you salary, director fees, or dividends, that income goes into your personal return. Do not use one TIN for both sides merely because you are the founder.

Suppose Nila's company earns BDT 200,000 from a client. She receives the money in her personal bank account and puts her personal TIN on the invoice. The records now suggest that Nila personally supplied the service, even though the contract and work belong to the company. The company's return, withholding certificate, and bank records will not reconcile.

---

## Why the Numbers Are Separate

Section 24 of the Companies Act makes an incorporated company a separate legal body. It owns its assets, debts, contracts, and accounts. Section 166 of the Income Tax Act requires a company to file a return, so it needs its own TIN.

The same section requires a shareholder director to file a personal return. Section 264 also requires proof of return submission when becoming a director or sponsor shareholder. Both sides therefore have work:

```text
Company business → company TIN and company return
Founder's personal income → personal e-TIN and personal return
```

See [e-TIN: Whose Name to Use and How to Apply](/en/tax/e-tin) for the application process and the difference between a TIN and proof of return submission.

## Which TIN Belongs to Which Transaction?

| Event | Name on the record | TIN |
|---|---|---|
| Company supplies goods or services to a client | Company | Company TIN |
| Company buys software or pays office costs | Company | Company TIN, if requested by the supplier |
| Company pays the founder a salary | Company expense and founder's personal income | Separate records for each side |
| Company declares a dividend | Company decision and shareholder's personal income | Recipient's personal TIN |
| Founder consults personally outside the company | Founder | Personal TIN |
| Founder puts personal funds into the company | Capital, share subscription, or loan, as approved | Record under the appropriate company account |

The last transaction is not a sale. Do not record a BDT 100,000 founder contribution as company revenue. Use the board decision, share papers, or loan record to classify it.

## Ask Three Questions When Someone Says “Send the TIN”

If a bank, buyer, authority, or online form merely asks for “TIN,” ask:

1. Is the record in the individual's or the company's name?
2. Is a TIN certificate enough, or is proof of return submission required?
3. Which assessment year's proof is required?

Section 264 of the Income Tax Act requires proof of return submission for various activities, including trade licensing in a city corporation or municipality, tenders, and import-export registration. Do not assume an old TIN certificate is sufficient.

The current Mushak-2.1 form requests e-TIN “where applicable.” Give the information of the person obtaining the BIN. Do not put a director's personal TIN into the company's field.

## Create Four Records After Incorporation

1. **Company TIN:** Reconcile the RJSC name, registration number, and address.
2. **Company bank account:** Arrange to receive customer payments there. Ask the bank for its current document list.
3. **Separate ledger:** Distinguish sales, expenses, founder contributions, and payments to founders.
4. **Return calendar:** Record the assessment year, responsible person, and proof of submission for the company and every shareholder director.

If a founder pays a company cost before the bank account opens, keep the receipt and write down why. The company can later identify whether a repayment is an expense reimbursement or loan repayment.

---

## Run a Monthly Reconciliation

Place three records side by side:

- Client contract and invoice
- Bank account that received the money
- Entity whose sales ledger records the transaction

All three should name the same entity. Correct the record promptly if the contract belongs to the company, the money went to a founder, and the invoice names another business.

## Common Mistakes

- Issuing company invoices under a personal TIN
- Receiving company revenue personally without recording it
- Treating a TIN certificate as proof of return submission
- Recording a founder contribution as a sale
- Putting a director's personal TIN into the company-TIN field
- Treating personal and company returns as one filing

## Checklist

- [ ] Obtained a separate TIN in the company's name
- [ ] Know the personal TIN and return status of every shareholder director
- [ ] Use the same entity name on contracts, invoices, banking, and tax records
- [ ] Record money contributed by or paid to a founder separately
- [ ] Confirm whether a counterparty needs the TIN or proof of return submission
- [ ] Maintain separate calendars for company and personal returns

Rule of thumb: **Use the same name on the contract, invoice, bank transaction, and tax record.**
