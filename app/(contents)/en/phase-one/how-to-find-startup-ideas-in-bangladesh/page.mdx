# Practical Ways to Find Startup Ideas in Bangladesh

> **In short:** A great startup idea doesn't just appear out of nowhere. Looking closely at real-life problems, and using technology to organize the sectors that are still messy — that's where most successful startup ideas in Bangladesh come from.

Stuck because you have no idea yet? Ideas don't fall from the sky; you go looking for them. Most good startup ideas in Bangladesh hide in the five places below. Here's the one-glance version, then we'll take each in turn.

## At a glance: where to look for ideas

| Source | Where the opportunity is | Bangladeshi example |
|---|---|---|
| Unorganized sectors | No structure, middlemen-heavy, done by hand | Truck Lagbe |
| Local adaptation | Works abroad, hasn't arrived or fit here | Pathao |
| B2B inefficiency | Business pain nobody has fixed | ShopUp |
| Your own problem | A daily frustration you're solving for yourself | Chaldal |
| Regulatory or tech waves | A new rule or technology opening a door | Fintech, AI |

## 1. Focus on unorganized sectors

A big part of Bangladesh's economy is still unorganized, or informal. Wherever you see no real structure, heavy reliance on middlemen, or everything done by hand — that's where a startup opportunity hides.

- **Example:** Hiring a truck used to be a huge hassle. Truck Lagbe organized that messy sector into an app.
- **Look for:** How technology can bring order to agricultural supply chains, local transport, property brokerage, or household services (plumbers, cleaners).

## 2. Take a global model and make it local

Many models that succeeded in developed countries haven't reached Bangladesh yet, or arrived and didn't fit. Rather than copying a foreign model outright, adapting it to the psychology and infrastructure of Bangladeshi people can be a brilliant idea.

- **Example:** Pathao started on Uber's model, but quickly realized motorbikes beat cars in Dhaka traffic. That local twist is what grew them.
- **Look for:** Which startups are working in developing countries like India, Indonesia, or Latin America that could also work here.

## 3. Hunt for inefficiencies in B2B

Most new founders only want to solve problems for ordinary consumers (B2C). But there's plenty of pain inside big companies and small-to-medium businesses (SMEs) too — solve it and they'll happily pay.

- **Example:** ShopUp became one of the country's largest startups by fixing supply-chain and logistics problems for small Facebook sellers and grocery shops.
- **Look for:** What trouble businesses run into keeping accounts, managing staff, or sourcing raw materials.

## 4. Solve your own problem

What problem do you hit in daily life or at work that you're already trying to solve yourself? If you face it, chances are thousands of others face the same thing.

- **Example:** Chaldal's founders got the idea for online grocery delivery from the sheer frustration of fighting Dhaka traffic to buy groceries.
- **Look for:** What was the most annoying task in your day today? Could software or a service make it easy?

## 5. Ride a regulatory or tech wave

A new government rule or a shift in technology often opens the door to new businesses.

- **Example:** When the government began encouraging digital payments, it opened huge opportunities for fintech. And the arrival of AI is now creating openings in edtech and customer support.
- **Look for:** Which recent change in a rule, a technology, or a habit is creating a new need.

## Idea-filtering checklist

Once you have a few ideas, ask yourself:

- [ ] Does this problem affect many people (at least a few hundred thousand)?
- [ ] Does it happen repeatedly — daily, weekly, or monthly?
- [ ] Are people spending money on it now, or willing to?
- [ ] Do I or my team have the skills to actually build this?
