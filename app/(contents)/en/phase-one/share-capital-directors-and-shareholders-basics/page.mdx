# Share Capital, Directors, and Shareholders Basics

> **Key Takeaway:** The numbers you write into the MoA/AoA (authorized capital, number of directors and shareholders) have real consequences later, at the bank, when hiring foreign staff, and when shares change hands. Understanding this mechanics upfront avoids getting stuck later.

## 1. Authorized vs. Paid-up Capital

- **Authorized Capital:** The maximum value of shares the company is allowed to issue. It's stated in the MoA and doesn't all need to be issued at once.
- **Paid-up Capital:** The amount shareholders have actually put into the company in exchange for shares.

For a local company, there's no legally mandated minimum for authorized or paid-up capital. But getting work permits for foreign employees usually requires the company's paid-up capital to be at least around USD 100,000, so keep that figure in mind if you're planning to hire foreign staff.

## 2. Directors

A private limited company needs at least 2 directors, who can be local or foreign nationals. Appointing or changing a director requires notifying RJSC via Form XII (see [Board Meetings, AGM, and Minutes Book Basics](../phase-one/board-meetings-agm-and-minutes-basics) for the filing deadline).

Removing a director against their will may require a shareholder resolution or, in some cases, court intervention, so it's worth keeping the director-removal rules clear in the AoA.

## 3. Shareholders

A private limited company can have a minimum of 2 and a maximum of 50 shareholders (excluding employee-shareholders). Cross that limit and the company is no longer "private." If you're bringing in many people through an ESOP or a small angel round, keep count of this number.

- **Allotment:** After issuing new shares, you must file a return of allotment with RJSC on Form XV within 60 days.
- **Transfer:** Moving existing shares from one shareholder to another requires filling out Form 117 (Instrument of Transfer), paying stamp duty at a set rate on the share value, and updating the company's share register and share certificates.

## Common Mistakes

- Keeping paid-up capital too low despite planning to hire foreign staff, which causes problems at work-permit time.
- Not filing Form XV within 60 days of issuing new shares.
- Treating a share transfer as done on a verbal agreement, without Form 117, stamp duty, or an updated share register, which causes ownership disputes later.
- Losing track of the shareholder count through an angel round or ESOP and crossing the 50-shareholder limit.

## Checklist

- [ ] Authorized capital matches your actual plans
- [ ] Paid-up capital requirement known if you're planning to hire foreign staff
- [ ] Director and shareholder counts within legal limits (2+ directors, 2-50 shareholders)
- [ ] Plan in place to file Form XV within 60 days of any new share issuance
- [ ] Correct share transfer process (Form 117, stamp duty, register update) understood
