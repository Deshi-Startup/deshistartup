# Memorandum and Articles of Association Basics

> **Key Takeaway:** The MoA (Memorandum of Association) is the company's constitution. It states what the company can and cannot do. The AoA (Articles of Association) is the internal rulebook. It states how the board and shareholders make decisions. Copying either from a template without understanding it causes problems later, when raising investment, applying for a license, or resolving a shareholder dispute.

MoA and AoA are mandatory documents for RJSC company registration. For the full registration process, see [Step-by-Step Private Limited Company Registration](../private-limited-company-registration-step-by-step).

## 1. What the Memorandum of Association (MoA) Contains

The MoA typically covers:

- **Name clause:** The company's official name, which must match the RJSC name clearance exactly and, for a private limited company, end in "Private Limited" or its Bangla equivalent.
- **Registered office clause:** Which district/division the company's registered office is in.
- **Objects clause:** A written statement of which businesses the company can operate in. Operating outside this clause can be legally questionable, and this clause gets checked when opening a bank account, applying for a sector-specific license, or raising investment.
- **Liability clause:** Usually "limited by shares," meaning a shareholder's liability is capped at what they've invested in shares.
- **Capital clause:** The company's authorized capital and how many shares it's divided into.

**Practical tip:** Write the objects clause for where the business is headed, not just what it does today. Too narrow a clause means amending the MoA later just to add a new line of business.

## 2. What the Articles of Association (AoA) Contains

The AoA governs how the company actually runs day to day: board meeting quorum, share transfer rules, appointing or removing directors, and how decisions get made. A private limited company's AoA must restrict share transfers, since restricting transferability and not inviting the public to subscribe for shares is what makes a company "private" in the first place.

Schedule I of the Companies Act, 1994 provides a set of model regulations (known as Table A) that can be adopted into the AoA. However, certain regulations (numbers 78, 79, 80, 81, and 82) don't automatically apply to a private company unless it's a subsidiary of a public company.

Changing the AoA later requires a special resolution under Section 87 (at least 21 days' notice, 75% support from members present and voting). See [Board Meetings, AGM, and Minutes Book Basics](../board-meetings-agm-and-minutes-basics) for details.

## 3. Match the AoA to Your Founder Agreement

Don't treat this as a legal formality only. Write the AoA to reflect what will actually happen. If co-founders have agreed to vesting or a right of first refusal on share transfers, the AoA should reflect that, or the founder agreement and the AoA's rules will conflict. See [Founder Agreement Basics](../founder-agreement-basics) for details.

## Common Mistakes

- Copying the objects clause word-for-word from another company's template, so it doesn't match the actual business and causes problems with licenses or investment later.
- Using a template AoA that's missing the share transfer restriction clause.
- Leaving the AoA out of sync with what the founder agreement actually says about vesting or board control, which becomes a dispute later.
- Not budgeting for stamp duty, which scales with authorized capital.

## Checklist

- [ ] The MoA's name clause matches the RJSC name clearance exactly
- [ ] The objects clause matches the current and near-future business
- [ ] The AoA includes a share transfer restriction clause
- [ ] Board quorum and decision-making rules match the founder agreement
- [ ] MoA/AoA reviewed by a company secretary or corporate lawyer
