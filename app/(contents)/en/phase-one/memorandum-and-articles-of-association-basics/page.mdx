# MoA and AoA: What to Understand Before Signing

> **Key Takeaway:** The MoA states the company's name, activities, share capital, and the limit of shareholder liability. The AoA governs internal decisions: directors, meeting quorum, voting, and share transfers. These are not merely files to upload to RJSC. A rule you sign without understanding may become a problem when the co-founders disagree, an investor comes in, or someone wants to leave.

Suppose Rafi and Nabila incorporate with equal shares. Their AoA does not say what happens when their votes are tied. Six months later, one wants to hire an employee and the other does not. The company has work and cash, but no decision. It is cheaper to write a deadlock process before registration than during the dispute.

A private-company registration application includes both the MoA and AoA. See [Step-by-Step Private Limited Company Registration](../phase-one/private-limited-company-registration-step-by-step) for the full application process.

---

## The MoA Sets the Company's Outer Boundary

The Memorandum of Association, or MoA, records the company's basic identity and scope. Find at least these five matters in the draft:

- **Name:** Does it exactly match the name clearance?
- **Registered office:** Does it state that the registered office will be in Bangladesh?
- **Objects:** What products or services will the company actually provide?
- **Liability:** Is shareholder liability limited to the unpaid amount on their shares?
- **Share capital:** What is the authorised capital, how many shares make it up, and what is each share worth?

Limited by shares does not simply mean “the amount already invested.” It generally limits a shareholder's liability to the amount still unpaid on the shares. For example, Rafi takes 1,000 shares with a face value of BDT 10 and pays BDT 6 per share. The unpaid amount is:

```text
1,000 × (10 - 6) = BDT 4,000
```

Even if the company's debts are greater, his ordinary liability as a shareholder is BDT 4,000. A personal guarantee, the person's own fraud, or another separate liability can change that result.

### A Common Objects-Clause Mistake

Some founders copy a long list from another company. Others write only today's single activity. The first version hides what the business really does. The second may leave the next adjacent product outside the stated scope.

Suppose you now build bookkeeping software for shops and expect to add inventory and payment features for the same customers. The draft should cover those foreseeable, related activities. A vague list that tries to cover anything imaginable is not the answer either. Writing an activity in the MoA does not replace a sector licence.

## The AoA Sets the Internal Decision Rules

The Articles of Association, or AoA, governs how people inside the company make decisions. Read the draft until you can answer these questions:

- Who may issue new shares, and with whose approval?
- If someone wants to sell shares, who must receive the first offer?
- Who can appoint or remove a director?
- Who calls a board meeting, and how many people make a quorum?
- Which matters use an ordinary vote, and which require greater consent?
- How will the founders break a voting deadlock?
- How will the company decide whether to declare a dividend?
- Who handles notices, signatures, and company records?

The Companies Act requires a private company to restrict the right to transfer its shares and prohibits it from inviting the public to subscribe. A restriction does not mean shares can never be sold. It means the transfer must follow the process in the AoA.

---

## Equal Founders Need Three Written Answers

When two founders have equal shares, do not leave these questions blank:

1. Which daily decisions may one founder make alone?
2. Which major decisions require both founders' consent?
3. If they cannot agree, will they use discussion, a neutral third party, a share sale, or another process?

For example, the responsible founder might approve spending within an authorised budget up to BDT 50,000. Both founders might need to approve a new share issue, BDT 500,000 in borrowing, or discontinuing the principal product. A 30-day deadlock might first go to a neutral mediator. These are examples, not recommended rules. Set the amounts and process for your business.

## Reconcile the AoA with the Founder Agreement

A founder agreement may cover vesting, what happens to shares when someone leaves, and who controls particular decisions. If the AoA says something different, a dispute creates a second argument about how the documents work together.

Place the documents side by side and complete a reconciliation table:

| Decision | Founder agreement | AoA | Conflict? |
|---|---|---|---|
| New shares |  |  |  |
| Share sale |  |  |  |
| Director change |  |  |  |
| Major spending or borrowing |  |  |  |
| A founder leaves |  |  |  |
| Voting deadlock |  |  |  |

If you find a conflict, do not finalise it by changing a few words yourself. Show both documents to a company secretary or lawyer who works with company law.

## Can the Documents Be Changed Later?

Yes, but “copy now and fix later” is a poor plan. Section 20 of the Companies Act allows a company to alter its AoA through a special resolution. Section 87 sets the notice and voting requirements for that resolution. Depending on the change, an RJSC filing and fee may follow.

MoA changes use different rules for different matters. Do not assume that changing the objects, name, registered office, and capital is one process. Check the current law and RJSC instructions before approving a change.

---

## Checklist Before Signing

- [ ] The name clearance and MoA use the same spelling
- [ ] The objects match the real and near-term business
- [ ] I understand the liability and share-capital figures
- [ ] Every subscriber's share count is correct
- [ ] I understand the share-transfer process
- [ ] I understand the rules for directors, meetings, voting, and deadlock
- [ ] The AoA has been reconciled with the founder agreement
- [ ] Irrelevant copied activities and rules have been removed
- [ ] A professional has explained anything I still do not understand

Rule of thumb: **Do not sign a provision that you cannot explain in your own words.**
