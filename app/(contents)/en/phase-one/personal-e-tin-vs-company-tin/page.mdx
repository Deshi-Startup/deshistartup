# Personal e-TIN vs Company TIN

> **Key Takeaway:** Your personal e-TIN and your company's TIN are two completely separate things. The moment a company gets its RJSC incorporation certificate, it becomes a distinct legal entity, so it needs its own TIN; you can't run the company's bank account, trade license, or contracts on a founder's personal TIN. Getting this distinction straight early avoids getting stuck later at the bank, at BIN registration, or in a tender.

## 1. Why the company needs its own TIN

The moment a private limited company receives its certificate of incorporation, it becomes a separate legal person under the Companies Act, distinct from its founders or directors. Because of this, NBR treats the company as a separate taxpayer and issues it its own TIN, which is not the same as any director's personal e-TIN.

In practice, the company's TIN should be obtained right after RJSC incorporation, because the trade license application, VAT/BIN registration, and opening the company's bank account all require the company's own TIN certificate. (For the step-by-step registration process, see the [e-TIN Guide](../phase-one/e-tin-guide).)

## 2. What a personal e-TIN is actually used for

A founder's personal e-TIN covers everything related to income tax in their own name, not the company's:

- Filing a personal income tax return (on salary, dividends, or other personal income)
- Submitting TIN as part of KYC when opening a personal bank account, making investments, or buying savings certificates
- Personal transactions such as property registration or vehicle registration
- Being a required credential before someone can even become a company director, since every director needs their own valid personal e-TIN

## 3. What a company TIN is actually used for

The company's TIN certificate is required for every formal transaction done in the company's name:

- Filing the company's own income tax return (a completely separate filing from the founder's personal return)
- Opening and activating the company's bank account, which the bank checks alongside the RJSC documents and trade license
- Trade license and other RJSC-related filings
- Applying for VAT/BIN registration (the TIN certificate has to be submitted with the BIN application; see the [VAT/BIN Guide](../phase-one/vatbin-guide) for details)
- Bidding on government tenders or registering as a vendor/supplier in B2B contracts, since almost every buyer or tender committee wants to see the vendor's company TIN, not a personal one

## 4. The common founder mistake: running the company on a personal TIN

Many founders keep operating on old habits for the first few months after incorporation, giving suppliers their personal TIN details or moving company money through a personal account. This causes problems because:

- Banks, BIN registration, and tender committees won't accept applications without a company TIN, so things get stuck
- Routing company income and expenses through a personal TIN creates messy books, which causes trouble later during an audit or return filing
- Mixing personal and company financial liability risks weakening the "corporate veil" that protects the separate legal entity

## Common Mistakes

- Delaying the company TIN application after RJSC incorporation, which then blocks the trade license, BIN, or bank account.
- Assuming that a director having a personal TIN covers the company's own tax obligations.
- Submitting a personal TIN to a supplier, client, or tender where a company TIN was requested.
- Running the company's first few months of transactions through a personal bank account and personal TIN.

## Checklist

- [ ] Company TIN application filed right after receiving the RJSC incorporation certificate
- [ ] Every director has a valid personal e-TIN
- [ ] Company bank account, trade license, and BIN applications use the company TIN, not a personal one
- [ ] Company TIN certificate ready for vendor registration in tenders or B2B contracts
- [ ] Plan in place to file the personal and company income tax returns separately
