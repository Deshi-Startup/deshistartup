# When VAT Registration is Required

> **Key Takeaway:** VAT registration (BIN) becomes mandatory through two separate paths. First, certain categories of business are required to register from day one, no matter how small their turnover is: import/export, supply against government tenders or work orders, and a handful of specific service sectors. Second, every other business only becomes required to register once its annual turnover crosses a threshold. Founders who conflate the two often assume "my turnover is small, so I don't need VAT yet," when in reality their business's category alone can make a BIN mandatory from the first taka of revenue.

This guide exists to help you answer one question: "do I need to register for VAT right now?" For the actual registration steps, portal, and paperwork once you've decided you need to register, see the [BIN/VAT Registration Guide](../phase-one/vatbin-guide). For the exact current threshold amounts and how the turnover-tax regime works, see [Turnover Tax and VAT Threshold Explainer](../phase-one/turnover-tax-and-vat-threshold-explainer). This page deliberately avoids stating specific Taka figures, since thresholds shift with each year's Finance Act.

## 1. Businesses Where Registration is Mandatory Regardless of Turnover

Under Section 4(2) of the Value Added Tax and Supplementary Duty Act, 2012, businesses engaged in certain economic activities must register no matter what their turnover is. The categories that recur most often are:

- **Import/export businesses:** clearing customs, opening an L/C, or handling shipping documentation for imports or exports requires a BIN, no matter how small the sales volume.
- **Supply against government or corporate tenders and work orders:** if you supply goods or services under a contract, work order, or through a tender, registration is mandatory from the very first contract.
- **Construction, consultancy, and supervisory firms:** construction contractors, engineering or management consultancies, and supervision service providers fall into this category.
- **Procurement providers and human resource supply/management organizations:** staffing, outsourcing, and manpower supply services.
- **ITES (Information Technology Enabled Services):** when supplied under a contract or work order.
- **Clearing and forwarding (C&F) agents.**
- **Manufacture, supply, or import of goods or services subject to Supplementary Duty (SD):** items like cigarettes, carbonated beverages, and certain luxury goods.
- **Specific service sectors NBR has designated by SRO:** telecom, banking and insurance, larger hotel/restaurant grades, advertising firms, and certain digital services are separately made mandatory through NBR orders.

**Caveat:** this list is not exhaustive. NBR's full list is long, spread across multiple SROs, and gets revised almost every year in the Finance Act, with categories added or dropped. Confirm whether your specific business type falls on the current list by checking recent [NBR](https://nbr.gov.bd) notifications or asking a VAT consultant or CA. Assuming you're exempt and getting it wrong can mean penalties and interest that cost far more than registering would have.

## 2. For Everyone Else: The Turnover-Based Rule

If your business doesn't fall into a mandatory category above, whether you need to register depends on your annual turnover. The general structure works like this:

- Below a certain threshold, neither VAT nor turnover tax applies.
- Within a middle band, you can typically enlist for a simplified turnover-tax regime instead of full VAT registration.
- Above an upper threshold, full VAT registration (a BIN) and charging VAT at the standard rate become mandatory.

For the exact current amounts, and how the turnover-tax regime actually works, read [Turnover Tax and VAT Threshold Explainer](../phase-one/turnover-tax-and-vat-threshold-explainer). These thresholds shift almost every budget cycle, which is why no figures are given here, pointing instead to a guide that will be kept current.

## 3. Why You Might Need to Register Even When It's Not Mandatory: The B2B Input-VAT Chain

Even without a legal obligation, many startups register for VAT early because their clients require it. The reasoning behind this is worth understanding:

- If your client is itself a VAT-registered company, it wants to claim an "input tax credit" for the VAT it pays on what it buys from you. Claiming that credit requires a valid Mushak 6.3 invoice, which only a fully VAT-registered supplier can issue.
- Businesses under the turnover-tax regime cannot issue Mushak 6.3 invoices and cannot claim input VAT rebates themselves. For B2B-dependent businesses (agencies, suppliers, subcontractors), this becomes a real practical barrier, corporate clients often simply won't work with a supplier that lacks a full BIN.
- Opening an L/C at a bank, participating in a government tender, or getting onto a large corporate's vendor panel often requires a BIN as a practical condition, even where it isn't a strict legal requirement for your category or turnover.

So when deciding, don't just ask "am I legally required to?" Also ask "what will my clients and partners require?"

## 4. Voluntary Registration: Benefits and Obligations

Even if none of the above makes registration mandatory, you can choose to register voluntarily. Weigh both sides before doing so:

- **Benefits:** access to more B2B and government work, the ability to claim input VAT rebates, and more credibility with large clients and banks.
- **Obligations:** a monthly VAT return every month, even with zero sales, correctly issuing and retaining a Mushak 6.3 invoice for every sale, and your books becoming subject to routine VAT office scrutiny.

For the details of invoicing and documentation, see [Mushak 6.3 Invoice and VAT Documentation Basics](../phase-one/mushak-63-invoice-and-vat-documentation-basics). What the monthly filing workflow looks like after registration is covered in a companion guide that is still being written.

## 5. A Quick Decision Checklist for Your Own Situation

Work through this sequence to place yourself quickly:

1. Does my business involve import, export, supply under a government or corporate tender, or one of the specific sectors listed above? If yes, I need a BIN today, regardless of turnover.
2. If not, has my annual turnover crossed, or is it approaching, the upper VAT threshold? If yes, full VAT registration is mandatory.
3. If not, does my turnover fall within the turnover-tax band? If yes, I have the option to enlist for turnover tax instead of full VAT.
4. If none of the above applies, do my main clients include VAT-registered corporates or government bodies that will expect a Mushak 6.3 invoice or a BIN? If yes, consider voluntary registration.

Wherever you're uncertain at any step, it's safer to confirm your classification with a VAT consultant or chartered accountant before deciding in writing, since the penalty for misclassifying yourself is usually far more expensive than registering would have been.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming VAT doesn't apply just because turnover is small, without checking whether the business's category is mandatory
- Starting import/export or tender supply without a BIN, then getting stuck at L/C or customs clearance later
- Promising Mushak 6.3 invoices to a corporate client while under the turnover-tax regime, which isn't actually possible
- Relying on an old blog post or a previous year's threshold figures without checking whether the budget has since changed them
- Deciding to register without first accounting for the real workload of monthly returns and documentation

## Checklist

- [ ] Checked whether the business's category falls under NBR's turnover-independent mandatory list
- [ ] Confirmed whether the business is involved in import/export or government/corporate tender supply
- [ ] Calculated where annual turnover stands, and how close it is to any threshold
- [ ] Checked whether key clients or partners will require a BIN or Mushak 6.3 invoices
- [ ] Confirmed the business's category with a VAT consultant or CA when in doubt
