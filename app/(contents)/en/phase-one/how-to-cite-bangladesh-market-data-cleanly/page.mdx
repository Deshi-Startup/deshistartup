# How to Cite Bangladesh Market Data Cleanly

Market data in Bangladesh startup content gets outdated quickly. Do not write claims like “Bangladesh has X crore users” without showing the source, date, definition, and caveat behind the number.

## 1. Use a Source Hierarchy

Not every source is equal. Use the strongest available source for the claim.

| Claim type | Check first | Use secondary sources when |
|---|---|---|
| GDP, inflation, labour, household data | BBS, Bangladesh Bank, World Bank | you need explanation or comparison |
| Mobile, internet, telecom subscribers | BTRC, operator/regulator publication | you need trend interpretation |
| Payment, banking, MFS, remittance | Bangladesh Bank, PSP/MFS circulars, annual reports | you need market commentary |
| Startup funding and ecosystem activity | Startup Bangladesh, BASIS, e-CAB, investor reports | private deal data needs cross-checking |
| Consumer behaviour and digital adoption | official surveys, DataReportal, reputable research firms | you can explain methodology or sample caveats |

## 2. Cite Claims Near the Claim

A good citation lets the reader verify the claim directly.

Weak:

> Bangladesh's e-commerce market is growing fast.

Better:

> When writing about Bangladesh's e-commerce market, specify whether the claim refers to B2C order volume, GMV, registered marketplaces, or digital payment volume.

Best:

> When citing “mobile internet subscribers,” check the regulator's definition, then include the source link and reporting month.

## 3. Do Not Use Data Without Dates

For market data, the date is part of the claim. Use this pattern:

- `Source: Bangladesh Bank, series/page name, checked 2026-07-05`
- `Source: BTRC subscriber report, reporting month, checked 2026-07-05`
- `Source: DataReportal Bangladesh report, publication year, checked 2026-07-05`

Update `last_verified` in frontmatter when a page's key factual claims are checked.

## 4. Do Not Draw Big Conclusions From One Source

If a report says “internet users increased,” that does not automatically mean “all founders should build app-first.” To turn data into practical advice:

1. read the source definition
2. check the methodology or sample
3. cross-check important claims against official or independent sources
4. include caveats in the advice

## 5. Common Mistakes

- linking only to a homepage instead of the exact report or notice
- presenting old data as current
- mixing up registered users, active users, subscribers, customers, and transaction volume
- using a news article as the authority for legal or regulatory truth
- omitting currency, year, and methodology from market size claims
- keeping AI-generated citations without manual checking

## 6. Suggested Citation Format

In Markdown:

```md
Check the relevant series on Bangladesh Bank's economic data page before writing the claim ([Bangladesh Bank Economic Data](https://www.bb.org.bd/en/index.php/econdata/index), checked 2026-07-05).
```

In the source section:

```md
## Relevant Sources

- [Bangladesh Bank Economic Data](https://www.bb.org.bd/en/index.php/econdata/index)
- [World Bank Data: Bangladesh](https://data.worldbank.org/country/bangladesh)
```

## Editorial Checklist

Before publishing, confirm:

- factual claims have sources
- source links are exact or as deep as possible
- data includes year or month
- source type is clear
- high-risk content uses official sources
- AI-generated citations were manually checked
