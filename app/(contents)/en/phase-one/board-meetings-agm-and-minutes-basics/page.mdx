# Board Meetings, AGM, and Minutes Book Basics

> **Key Takeaway:** Even a small private company must follow the rules for Annual General Meetings (AGMs), board meetings and minutes. The first AGM is generally due within 18 months of incorporation. A board meeting is required at least once every three months and at least four times a year. Record the decisions so you can support later RJSC filings and answer questions from banks or investors.

Many founders assume board resolutions and AGMs only matter for large, listed companies. Under the Companies Act, 1994, they apply to every private limited company as well.

## 1. When You Must Hold the AGM

Under Section 81 of the Companies Act, 1994:
- The first AGM must be held within 18 months of incorporation.
- After that, at least one AGM must be held every calendar year, and the gap between two AGMs cannot exceed 15 months.
- Failing to hold the AGM on time can carry a fine of up to Tk 10,000, plus Tk 250 per day for continuing default.
- Except for the first AGM, a company may apply to the Registrar within 30 days after the deadline expires. The Registrar may extend the deadline by up to 90 days or to 31 December of that calendar year, whichever comes first.

**What happens at the AGM:** The audited accounts and auditor's report are placed before shareholders. The meeting may also appoint the auditor and, where applicable, decide on dividends or directors whose terms are ending. Use the company's Articles of Association (AoA) to set the exact agenda.

## 2. How Often to Hold Board Meetings

Section 96 of the Companies Act, 1994 requires at least one board meeting every three months and at least four each year. The Act and the company's AoA together determine notice, quorum and voting rules.

- **AGM vs. board meeting:** Shareholders attend the AGM and approve major matters (accounts, auditor appointment). Only directors attend board meetings, which handle important operational decisions (approving large contracts, opening bank accounts, appointing officers).
- Section 95 requires written notice of each board meeting to be sent to every director who is in Bangladesh at their Bangladesh address. Keep evidence that the notices were sent.

## 3. Keeping a Minutes Book Is Mandatory

Section 89 of the Companies Act, 1994 requires a company to record proceedings of general meetings and board or board-committee meetings in books kept for that purpose. Section 178 concerns debentures, not meeting minutes.

- Each page should be initialled, with the final page dated and signed by the chair of that meeting or the next meeting. Properly signed minutes are treated as initial evidence of the proceedings.
- The general-meeting minutes book must be kept at the registered office. Under Section 90, members may inspect it during business hours without charge. The company may set reasonable restrictions but must allow at least two hours each day.
- That inspection right covers general-meeting minutes. The Act does not make board minutes open to every shareholder.

## 4. Types of Resolutions

- **Ordinary resolution:** Passed by a simple majority of votes cast and used for routine decisions.
- **Special resolution (Section 87):** The notice must give at least 21 days and state the intention to propose a special resolution. At least three-fourths of members present and voting must support it. Matters such as changing the name, altering the MoA or AoA, or reducing capital may require one, but the resolution alone may not complete the full legal process.
- **Board resolution:** Passed by directors in a board meeting, e.g., opening a bank account, authorizing signatories, approving a major contract. Banks and investors frequently ask for a copy of these.

What you must file with RJSC depends on the decision. For example, annual returns may include “Schedule X”, while a director change may require “Form XII”. See [RJSC Annual Returns and Filing Calendar](../phase-one/rjsc-annual-returns-and-filing-calendar). Late filings can lead to fines, notices and due-diligence gaps.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming that because the company is just family or friends, AGMs and minutes "aren't needed," and filing nothing for years.
- Making board decisions verbally with no written resolution or minutes. This becomes a problem when a bank or investor later asks for proof.
- Discovering right before a fundraise that three years of AGMs or minutes are missing. The document gap will surface in due diligence.

## Checklist

- [ ] First AGM held within 18 months of incorporation
- [ ] AGM held every calendar year, gap never exceeding 15 months
- [ ] Minutes of every AGM and board meeting written, pages initialled, and the chair's signature obtained
- [ ] Written board/special resolutions kept for major decisions (bank accounts, large contracts, share changes)
- [ ] You know which forms are due at RJSC after each AGM, and file them on time
