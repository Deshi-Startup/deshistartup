# Bangladesh Startup Ecosystem: Where to Start

> **TL;DR:** The ecosystem is the whole web of groups, events, associations, programmes, grants and investors. A new founder's job isn't to memorise the web – it's to knock on the right door at the right time. The order: join the community today, apply to programmes when your stage fits, and go to investors with customer proof in hand. Today's task is in step one.

Imagine Tanvir, up from Bogura for a startup event in Dhaka. A packed hall, familiar faces on stage, everyone seemingly knowing everyone. Standing in a corner with his tea, he thinks: this world belongs to other people. Yet every person in that room walked in alone for the first time once. Nobody is born into an ecosystem; everybody enters it. The only question is through which door, and when.

One thing to clear up first. This page shows you the doors, not the whole map. The full picture of who plays what role (government, payments, couriers, talent, media) is in the [ecosystem overview](/en/ecosystem), and the organised lists of programmes and investors live in the [directory](/en/directory).

## Step 1: join the community – today

You do not need a separate budget to start observing a community, though some events and memberships charge fees. The most valuable thing here is the experience of people who have already walked the road you're starting on.

- **Facebook groups:** join a founders' group like [BD Startup Founders](https://www.facebook.com/groups/bdstartupfounders). Read for the first week and pick up the room's language. Then ask questions – but specific ones. "I'm starting a new business, can anyone guide me?" will sink without a trace. "Has anyone done a trade license for homemade food in Chattogram? Which category, and how long did it take?" will get answers. A specific question shows you've done your homework, and that's exactly what earns people's time.
- **Your sector's association:** in software or IT services, that's [BASIS](https://basis.org.bd); in online commerce, [e-CAB](https://e-cab.net). Before joining, check their public event and training notices. Confirm with the organiser whether non-members may attend.
- **Event craft:** a real conversation with two people beats exchanging cards with fifty. Prepare two things before you go: a one-line answer to "so what do you do?", and one specific question of your own. If a conversation clicks, follow up the next day with a one-line message – most people never do, which is why the ones who do get remembered.

If you're shy, don't worry – it's actually an advantage. Networking isn't working the stage; it's one conversation worth remembering. That happens in the corner, tea in hand.

## Step 2: apply to programmes – when your stage fits

Incubator, accelerator, grant – if the words are new, check the [glossary](/en/start-here/glossary) first. The short version: incubators are for very early teams, while accelerators add speed to an already-running startup. Grants generally do not have to be repaid, but they do come with spending and reporting conditions.

On the government side, two names to know. The ICT Division's [iDEA project](https://idea.gov.bd) gives early-stage startups grants and training. And [BIG](https://big.gov.bd) (Bangabandhu Innovation Grant) is the national innovation competition whose winners receive grants. The amounts, cycles and eligibility rules change, so check their sites before applying. A curated list of private incubators and accelerators – including who takes which stage – is in the [accelerator directory](/en/directory/accelerators).

Before applying, ask yourself one question: does this programme unlock the exact place I'm stuck? If what you need is mentors, that's one programme; customers, another; money, a third. An accelerator may run for several weeks or months. If you join more than one programme, first check that you will still have enough time for the business. The full application process is walked through in [applying to an accelerator](/en/journeys/apply-to-an-accelerator).

## Step 3: go to investors – with proof in hand

Investors are the ecosystem's most talked-about door, but for a new founder they are usually a later one. Investors look for proof: orders, repeat customers and numbers that climb. Without that proof, an investment conversation is less likely to progress.

Names worth knowing: [Bangladesh Angels Network](https://bdangels.co), the country's network of angel investors, and [Startup Bangladesh Limited](https://startupbangladesh.vc), the government's venture capital company. Who invests at which stage and at what size is kept current in the [investor directory](/en/directory/investors), and the full map of money (from your own savings all the way to VC) is in the [funding roadmap](/en/funding).

One subtle point, though. Asking for money comes later, but *being known* doesn't. If you meet an investor at an event, don't launch into a pitch – describe the business in one line and ask what they're seeing in the market. Six months later, when you email with proof in hand, the line "we spoke at that event" is what opens the door.

## What to carry in your pocket

Never walk through any of these doors empty-handed. Draft these three things first:

- [ ] Wrote my one-line introduction: whose problem, which problem, how I'm solving it
- [ ] Filled in [the one-page plan](/en/start-here/one-page-startup-plan), so I can answer with numbers if someone probes
- [ ] Wrote down, in one line, the question I'm most stuck on right now

With these three in your pocket, you can speak clearly in a group thread, at an event or in a programme interview. People can also see where they may be able to help.

Rule of thumb: **community today, programmes when the stage fits, investors when there's proof.**
