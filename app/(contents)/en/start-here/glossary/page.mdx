# Startup Glossary: Bangla-English Startup Terms

> **In short:** The startup world is packed with English jargon, and one unfamiliar term can lock you out of the whole conversation. This page gives the plain-language meaning of those terms in one place, each with a Bangladeshi example and the taka maths, so the word sticks instead of just being memorised. You don't need to swallow it whole. Use find-in-page for the term you need, and where a concept deserves a deeper read, the link to its full guide sits right beside it.

Imagine Sumaiya at her first investor meeting for her software startup. The questions come fast: "What's your MRR? Your CAC-to-LTV ratio? How's churn?" She built the product with her own hands and knows her customers by name, yet she can't parse the questions. The meeting ends, and the late-night googling begins. This page is that night's reading, collected in one place ahead of time.

And when you hear an unfamiliar term in a meeting, stop and ask. It costs you nothing. Nodding along without understanding is the real danger, because the conditions hide inside the terms. Plenty of investment stories have come undone on exactly those hidden conditions.

If you're just starting out, you don't need to memorise the whole list. Get these few down first and let the rest come as you need them: MVP, product-market fit, traction, runway, burn rate, unit economics, CAC, LTV, equity and dilution. The last two matter most, because signing paper without understanding the ownership maths is a founder's most expensive mistake.

## From idea to product

- **Startup** – a new business model that isn't proven yet, but which can grow very fast once it works. A grocery shop runs about the same year after year; a startup either shoots up or shuts down. The full explanation is in [What is a startup?](/en/start-here/what-is-a-startup)
- **MVP (minimum viable product)** – the smallest thing that can test your riskiest assumption. Take a food-delivery idea: before building an app, take orders for 10 days through a Facebook page and a WhatsApp number. That's the MVP. Not a half-built bad product; a small but real test.
- **Prototype** – the first sample, built to show and to learn. Not for selling; for understanding. A screen sketched on paper or a demo built in Canva counts as a prototype too.
- **Beta** – a trial version released to a small group of customers, released precisely so the flaws surface before the wider market sees them.
- **Early adopter** – the customer who grabs new things first. They tolerate bugs, give sharp feedback, and are the most valuable teachers of the early days. It's their input that gets a product on its feet.
- **Hypothesis** – a belief like "people will pay to solve this problem" that, until you test it, is just a guess. A business is really the act of testing one assumption after another.
- **Pivot** – changing a major part of the business after learning from a test. The customer, problem, solution, price, or sales route may change. Not defeat; putting what you learned to use.
- **Product-market fit (PMF)** – demand strong enough within a defined market that customers keep using the product, buy again, and tell others. When the market starts pulling the thing out of your hands, you're close. No single metric proves it. The steps to get there are in the [Validation](/en/validation) section.
- **Moat** – an advantage that makes you hard for a competitor to overtake: a strong brand, cheaper supply, a large network, or accumulated data. Like the moat around a fort, the wider it is the safer you are. A new business has none; you have to build one.

## Customers and growth

- **B2B / B2C** – selling to businesses versus selling straight to consumers. Supplying soap to shops is B2B; selling to one buyer from your page is B2C. The sales style and the cash cycle differ between them.
- **Lead** – a potential customer who has shown interest. The person writing "dam koto?" in your inbox isn't a buyer yet; they're a lead. Turning leads into buyers is the real work of selling.
- **Conversion** – an interested person becoming a buyer, measured as a rate. 100 people ask the price and 8 buy: that's 8 percent conversion. Even a small change moves this rate.
- **Funnel** – the picture of the steps from seeing to buying, with people dropping off at each step. Like a funnel: many pour in at the top, a few come out the bottom. Seeing where the biggest drop is tells you where to work.
- **Traction** – hard evidence that the business is genuinely moving: growing orders, revenue, regular usage, or returning customers. Investors want less story and more traction.
- **North star metric** – the one number the whole team steers by, which rises only when customers actually get value. For food delivery it might be "orders delivered on time." Better to make one number real than to chase ten.
- **Retention** – the ability to keep customers. Of the people who bought this month, the share who come back next month. Keeping an old customer is far cheaper than winning a new one.
- **Cohort** – a group of customers who started together, say everyone who first bought in June. Tracking them month by month shows which group stays longest. The truth hidden behind an average shows up in the cohort. The maths is in the [Metrics](/en/metrics) section.
- **Churn** – retention's flip side: the rate at which customers leave. Think of a leaky bucket: pour in as much as you like, and if the hole is big it never fills. Spending on ads without fixing churn is pouring water into a leaky bucket.
- **Referral** – new customers arriving through old ones, spontaneously or for a small reward. A happy customer is the cheapest marketing there is.
- **K-factor / virality** – how many new customers, on average, each customer brings in. Above one, the business spreads on its own, without ads. It's referral turned into a number.
- **Organic vs paid** – customers who arrive without ad spend (search, shares, word of mouth) versus customers who came from ads. Paid gets a faster response at the start, but organic lasts longer.
- **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** – the average cost of winning one new customer. Spend 10,000 taka on boosting and get 20 new orders: CAC is 500 taka. When this cost exceeds what a customer is worth, the more you sell the more you lose.
- **LTV (lifetime value)** – the total profit a customer leaves in the business over the whole relationship. LTV is generally considered healthy at three times CAC or more. Reckon with the sale price alone and the real cost of serving that customer stays hidden.
- **TAM / SAM / SOM** – market size in three layers: the whole possible market, the part you can realistically serve, and what you'll actually capture in the first few years. Investors want the big dream and the realistic number side by side.

## Marketplace, page and e-commerce metrics

- **GMV (gross merchandise value)** – the total value of everything sold across a platform. Not the marketplace's own income; the total money changing hands. 50 lakh taka of orders in a month is 50 lakh GMV, though the platform may keep only a small slice.
- **Take rate** – the percentage a marketplace keeps as commission on each transaction. On 50 lakh GMV at a 10 percent take rate, the platform earns 5 lakh taka. This rate is the marketplace's actual business.
- **AOV (average order value)** – the average taka value per order. 2 lakh taka of sales across 400 orders is an AOV of 500 taka. Raise AOV and you earn more from the same customer.
- **Repeat rate** – the share of customers who buy more than once. 30 of 100 buyers come back: a 30 percent repeat rate. A low repeat rate means something in the product or service is off.
- **RTO / returned parcel (return to origin)** – a cash-on-delivery parcel that comes back because the customer refuses it. Each return puts two-way courier costs on you. High RTO is the biggest source of bleeding in Bangladeshi e-commerce. Ways to manage it are in the [Payments](/en/payments) section.

## The money maths

- **Revenue** – the total money that came in from sales, before any costs are deducted. Earning revenue and making a profit are not the same; many high-revenue businesses run at a loss.
- **COGS / direct cost (cost of goods sold)** – what it directly takes to make or source and sell a thing: materials, packaging, courier. Office rent and salaries don't count here. Subtract this from sales and you get gross profit.
- **Gross margin** – the percentage of sales left after direct cost. On a 400-taka jar of pickle with 220 taka of ingredients and packaging, gross profit is 180 taka and gross margin is 45 percent. When margin is thin, selling a lot still yields no profit.
- **Unit economics** – the profit-and-loss maths of a single unit: what's left on one order or one customer at the end. Lose money on one unit and a thousand units lose a thousand times as much. Settle this before you decide "scaling will make it profitable."
- **Burn rate** – how much more you spend than you earn each month. Earning 1 lakh and spending 3 lakh means a burn of 2 lakh taka. The higher the burn, the faster the cash in hand runs out.
- **Runway** – how many months you can survive on the money at hand: cash divided by burn. 10 lakh in the bank at 2 lakh monthly burn is a 5-month runway. Before it runs out you must either grow revenue or raise fresh money.
- **Break-even** – the sales level at which the month's costs are fully covered; profit starts beyond it. Knowing your costs and margin lets you work it out; the method is in [the one-page plan](/en/start-here/one-page-startup-plan).
- **Cash flow** – the stream of money in and out. You can be profitable on paper and still have no cash in hand if you sell on credit, and that's exactly when a business chokes.
- **Working capital** – the money on hand to run day to day: the liquidity to buy stock, pay salaries and cover rent. Sell on credit while buying stock for cash and this is the money that gets stuck.
- **Accounts receivable** – money you've earned by selling but haven't collected yet. In B2B, 30 to 60 days of credit is normal, which is why the profit in your books doesn't match the balance in your bank.
- **MRR / ARR (monthly / annual recurring revenue)** – income that arrives on schedule every month or year in subscription-style businesses. One-off sales don't count, which is why this income is more dependable.
- **Bootstrapping** – growing without outside investors, on your own savings and the business's own income. Slower going, but the ownership stays entirely in your hands.

## Funding and investors

For the whole system behind these terms, read the [funding roadmap](/en/funding).

- **Angel investor** – an individual investing small amounts of their own money at the earliest stage. Often brings advice and useful introductions along with the cheque. Many angels here are successful founders themselves.
- **VC (venture capital)** – a firm that pools other people's money into a fund and invests it in startups. It takes risk deliberately; one big win in ten pays for the whole fund. So a VC is looking for the chance to get very big, fast.
- **Pre-seed / seed / Series A** – the stages of funding rounds. Seed means exactly that: money to prove the idea. Series A comes after evidence that the model works, to scale it.
- **Pitch and pitch deck** – a pitch is the structured explanation you give an investor; a pitch deck is the short slide document shown with it. A good deck is short, clear, and question-raising.
- **Incubator** – a programme that helps an early team get on its feet with learning, mentors, workspace, or a network. The benefits and timelines vary from place to place.
- **Accelerator** – a programme that helps an operating team move faster over a fixed few months. Some provide money, some take equity in return, some offer training only. Read the terms carefully before applying.
- **Demo day** – the final day of a programme, when every team pitches in front of investors. Months of work riding on a single day.
- **Grant** – money that normally requires neither loan repayment nor equity. It can still carry conditions on the work, spending, and reporting; miss those and the money is held back.
- **Term sheet** – the draft of an investment's key terms: how much money, at what valuation, with what rights. Signing it isn't the final contract, but the direction of the negotiation is set here. Have a lawyer look at anything you don't understand before you sign.
- **Due diligence** – the investor's close inspection before releasing the money: your books, your paperwork, every claim you've made. Like verifying the deed before buying land. Keep your records tidy and this stage passes quickly.
- **Convertible note / SAFE** – an agreement that provides money now and turns into shares later under defined rules. When, at what price, and on what conditions it converts vary by document. It looks small but the future ownership hides inside it, so don't sign without legal advice.
- **Valuation cap** – the maximum valuation, fixed in advance in a SAFE or note, at which the earlier investor's money converts into shares. The lower the cap, the more shares that earlier investor gets.
- **Pro-rata** – the earlier investor's right to put in more money in the next round to hold their percentage. In a good company, this right is an investor's main path to a big return.
- **Liquidation preference** – the terms deciding who gets paid back first if the company is sold. If the sale price is low, the investor takes their money out first and the founder can be left with very little. The least-understood and most costly term on a term sheet.
- **Exit** – the event where an investor or founder turns their stake into cash, usually a company sale or a stock-market listing. This is where everyone's real gain or loss is settled.
- **Data room** – a folder gathering all the company's key documents in one place for due diligence. Organise it ahead of time and the investment moves faster.

## Equity and company paperwork

- **Equity** – an ownership share in the company. It's what investors get in exchange for their money. Giving up equity means giving up a share of future profit, so give it up with the maths in mind.
- **Share** – the unit ownership is measured in. Whatever fraction of total shares you hold, that's exactly your stake.
- **Valuation, pre-money and post-money** – the agreed price of the whole company. The price before the money goes in is pre-money; after the new money is added it's post-money. Put 25 lakh into a 1-crore pre-money and post-money is 1 crore 25 lakh, giving the investor a 20 percent stake. Be clear up front which one you're both talking about.
- **Dilution** – existing owners' percentages shrinking when new shares are issued. Your slice gets smaller, but if the company grows a smaller slice can be worth more. A small slice of a big pizza often beats a big slice of a small one.
- **Cap table** – the complete list of who holds how many shares. It's among the first things an investor asks to see. Keep it clean from the start and you save trouble later.
- **Vesting and cliff** – earning shares gradually rather than all at once, after meeting time or work conditions. The common rule is full vesting over four years, with nothing at all until you pass the one-year "cliff." The point is to stop a co-founder who leaves early from walking off with a full stake.
- **ESOP / option pool** – equity set aside so employees can have a future option to receive shares. It helps attract good people, but the pool is usually carved out of the founders' share.
- **MoA / AoA** – the company's two founding documents: what the company will do (MoA) and how it runs internally (AoA). Filed with RJSC at registration; details in [Company types](/en/company-types).
- **Authorized / paid-up capital** – authorized capital is the maximum value of shares the company may issue; paid-up capital is what shareholders have actually paid for the shares they hold. The two aren't the same and are often confused on the paperwork.
- **IP (intellectual property)** – the brand name, the logo, the software's code, your own recipe. Investors check whose name it's registered under during due diligence. Safer to hold it in the company's name than a founder's.
- **Compliance** – the recurring work of staying legal: filing returns, VAT records, licence renewals. Not a one-time task but an annual routine. Let it pile up and the fines pile up too.

## The acronyms you'll meet on the forms

Registration, tax and payment paperwork will put these acronyms in front of you again and again.

- **RJSC** – the Registrar of Joint Stock Companies, the government office for registering companies and partnerships. From name clearance to company registration, it all happens here.
- **NBR** – the National Board of Revenue, the top government body for tax and VAT. TIN, VAT, and customs duty all sit under it.
- **TIN / e-TIN** – the Tax Identification Number, your identity for paying income tax. The company needs its own and the owner needs a separate one. Details in [e-TIN, VAT and BIN](/en/e-tin-vat-bin).
- **VAT and BIN** – VAT is the value-added tax charged on goods and services, and BIN is the Business Identification Number taken to handle it. When it applies and what the threshold is are in [e-TIN, VAT and BIN](/en/e-tin-vat-bin).
- **Trade licence** – the basic permission to run a business, taken from the City Corporation or Union Parishad. It must be renewed every year; the steps are in [Trade licence](/en/trade-license).
- **MFS (mobile financial services)** – services like bKash, Nagad, and Rocket. Most of the country's digital transactions run through them.
- **Payment gateway** – the service that lets a website or app take money by card and MFS, like SSLCommerz or aamarPay. Check when the money reaches you and what fee is deducted before you commit. Details in the [Payments](/en/payments) section.
- **KYC (know your customer)** – the identity-verification rule. It's exactly why a bank, MFS, or payment gateway asks for your NID and papers before switching you on.
