# Startup, SME, Agency, and E-commerce: What Is the Difference?

> **In short:** The label you give your venture — startup, SME, agency, or e-commerce — is not just a word. It decides where you raise money, how fast you can expect to grow, what paperwork you need, and how you measure success. Pick the wrong label on day one and every decision after it bends the wrong way.

New founders in Bangladesh often blur what they are actually building. A neighbourhood shop calls itself a startup and wastes months chasing funding; a genuine tech product gets run like an ordinary service business and loses its shot at scale. Tell the four types apart and you stop making that mistake.

The difference isn't only in definitions — it's in the maths of money, speed, and risk. Here's the one-glance version, then we'll take each in turn.

## The four types at a glance

| Type | Where capital comes from | How it grows | When profit matters | Risk | Who it suits |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Startup | VCs / angels, in exchange for equity | Leaps — more customers doesn't mean proportionally more cost | Much later; growth first | Highest | Chasing a big market fast |
| SME | Savings, family, bank loans | Step by step, in a straight line | From day one | Moderate | Proven demand, steady income |
| Agency | Client advances | Only when you add people | Almost from the start | Low | Selling a specific skill |
| E-commerce / F-commerce | Own capital / bank | Costs rise as orders rise | Fast, if margins hold | Moderate — unsold stock | Selling products |

## 1. Startup

A startup is built to grow fast — to reach a lot of people in a short time. It bets on technology or a genuinely new model that hasn't been proven yet. Once the model works, ten times the customers doesn't cost ten times as much to serve. That is the real magic of a startup.

- **Examples:** [Pathao](/en/case-studies/pathao) (ride sharing), Shikho (edtech), ShopUp (B2B commerce), Chaldal (online grocery).
- **How it grows:** In leaps. Going from 1 customer to 10,000 does not raise costs proportionally.
- **Capital:** Usually from a VC (venture capital) firm or an angel investor. They put in money in exchange for equity (a share of ownership), betting the company will be worth many times more in a few years.
- **Risk:** The highest. Most startups don't survive, often because the market isn't ready yet.

## 2. SME or Regular Business

An SME is a traditional business built on proven demand in a local market. There's no new bet on the model — nobody needs proof that a restaurant can sell food.

- **Examples:** Restaurant, grocery store, boutique, pharmacy, small factory.
- **How it grows:** Step by step, in a straight line. Doubling revenue usually means doubling costs too — a new branch, more staff.
- **Capital:** Own savings, loans from family and friends, or a bank loan.
- **Risk:** Moderate, because the model is already familiar.

## 3. Agency or Service Business

In an agency you deliver a specific service to clients and get paid for it.

- **Examples:** Digital marketing agency, software firm, law firm, consultancy.
- **How it grows:** Only when you add people. More projects means more hires, which is why rapid scaling is hard.
- **Capital:** Can start on very little. Often a client's advance payment is enough to begin.
- **Risk:** Comparatively low — but income stops the moment you or the team stop working.

## 4. E-commerce or F-commerce

Selling products online — on your own website, or on a Facebook or Instagram page. Selling through Facebook is what people call F-commerce. In practice it's the digital face of an SME.

- **Examples:** A clothing or gadget page on Facebook, a jewellery shop on Instagram. (A marketplace like Daraz is different — that's a startup.)
- **How it grows:** In a straight line. Moving from 10 orders a day to 100 also raises packaging, courier, and support costs.
- **Capital:** Own capital or a bank loan.
- **Risk:** Moderate. The big fear is unsold stock — money stuck in inventory.

## Startup vs SME in the government's books

Each has its own official definition, and that definition decides which government benefits, loans, or funds you qualify for.

**Startup:** Under Bangladesh Bank's startup finance policy (as of 2025), a startup is a company with annual turnover under BDT 100 crore, less than 12 years old, that uses technology or a new idea with the potential to grow fast. You have to fit this definition to apply for the government startup fund or low-interest startup loans.

**SME:** Under the National Industrial Policy 2022, a business is sized on two things — the value of its fixed assets (excluding land and buildings) and its number of workers:

| Tier | Manufacturing (assets / workers) | Service (assets / workers) |
|---|---|---|
| Micro | BDT 10–75 lakh / 1–25 | Under BDT 10 lakh / up to 15 |
| Small | BDT 75 lakh–15 crore / 26–120 | BDT 10 lakh–2 crore / 16–50 |
| Medium | BDT 15–50 crore / 121–300 | BDT 2–30 crore / 51–120 |

Day to day, nobody checks what you call yourself. These definitions matter when you knock on the door of a government scheme or fund. The real question is always the same — which model are you actually running?

There's another practical difference: the paperwork you stand on. To raise VC money by selling equity you usually need a [private limited company](/en/company-types), and foreign VC money also needs Bangladesh Bank approval. A small SME or Facebook shop, on the other hand, often runs fine on a [trade licence](/en/trade-license) and a sole proprietorship. Tax and [VAT](/en/e-tin-vat-bin) also shift with the type and your turnover.

## Grey areas — where people get confused

The definitions are easy to memorise. The knots appear at the edges. A few common ones:

- **I sell on a Facebook page — am I a startup?** Usually not. Buying products and selling them online is F-commerce, the digital form of an SME. It becomes a startup only when you build a tool or platform for sellers that spreads fast through technology.
- **Can an agency ever become a startup?** Yes — if you step out of selling services and build a product. Many software startups are born this way: doing client work, building a tool along the way, then turning that tool into a separate product sold to many customers. This is called productizing a service.
- **I run a direct-to-consumer (D2C) brand — SME or startup?** It depends on how you want to grow. Your own money, step by step, staying profitable — that's an SME. Technology, brand, and outside investment to capture a national or global market fast — that behaves like a startup.
- **Does an SME grow into a startup?** Not by itself. Getting big and being a startup are not the same thing — the difference is in the growth model and the funding. A profitable restaurant chain can get huge and still be an SME.

## Which one is right for you?

- Want to grow fast, raise big investment, and take extreme risk — **startup**.
- Want profit from day one and to grow steadily on your own terms — **SME or e-commerce**.
- Want to sell a specific skill (coding, design, marketing) and start on little capital — **agency**.

Before you decide, ask yourself three honest questions:

1. Can my model spread fast to a big market through technology, or does it serve one area or one client at a time?
2. Do I want profit from the start, or am I willing to lose money for a few years to grow first?
3. Will I take outside investment, or keep full ownership on my own money?

If the answers don't line up, you may be forcing one type into another's clothes. Remember: none of these is inherently smaller or greater. It all comes down to your goal, your capital, and your appetite for risk.
