# One-Page Startup Plan

> **TL;DR:** A business plan is for thinking, not for show. Copy the grid below into a notebook or a Google Doc, set a timer, and fill in the twelve boxes within half an hour. The box you get stuck on is your most urgent homework. Then come back once a month and change whatever has been proven wrong.

You know the problem with a forty-page business plan? It takes a month to write, nobody reads it, and among all those pages the hard questions have room to hide. On one page there is nowhere to hide. Either the answers to "who is the customer" and "where does the money come from" exist, or they don't – and you can see it at a glance.

The idea isn't ours. Founders worldwide use a one-page grid called the Lean Canvas, and Y Combinator's Startup School teaches the same habit of keeping plans small. The grid below is the same idea in a deshi shape, sized for a small Bangladeshi business.

## Copy the grid

```text
One-page startup plan – [business name] – [date]

Customer and problem
1. Problem: whose pain, and which one? (one line)
2. First customers: who are the first 100? Where do I find them?
3. Current workaround: how do people handle this today?

Product and market
4. Solution: what exactly am I selling? (one line)
5. Why switch: why leave the current workaround for mine?
6. Channel: where will customers find me?

Money
7. Price: what am I charging?
8. Margin per sale: price minus direct costs = ?
9. Monthly costs: what goes out every month, sales or no sales
10. Break-even: how many sales a month cover the costs? (no. 9 ÷ no. 8)

Next steps
11. Riskiest assumption: which one, if wrong, breaks everything?
    How will I test it?
12. Three tasks for the next 30 days:
```

One rule for filling it in: two lines per box, maximum. Wanting to write more means the answer isn't clear yet.

## The boxes people get stuck on

**Boxes 1 to 3 (customer and problem):** don't start with the solution – that's the trap everyone falls into. "I'll build an app" is easy to write, but if you can't say whose pain it reduces, the other eleven boxes are meaningless. Stuck here? Take a pass through [idea validation](/en/validation) first.

**Boxes 3 and 5 are a pair:** however people handle the job today (a paper ledger, the familiar shop, or simply not doing it) – that is your real competitor. Box 5 must answer against exactly that: where are you better – price, time, trust, or reach?

**Box 10, break-even:** the number of sales that covers the month's costs, after which profit begins. It works like a rickshaw's daily rental: until the driver has paid the owner's 500-taka daily deposit, every trip only pays off the deposit – income starts with the trip after that. This box tells you your business's "daily deposit." If the number comes out absurdly large (you'd need 500 orders a month in a market of 200 buyers), the plan just saved you – the price, the costs, or the market has to change.

**Box 11, the riskiest assumption:** the most valuable box on the page. Every business stands on a few assumptions, and one of them is always the shakiest. If that one breaks first, all the other work is wasted. So test that single assumption before anything else, as cheaply as possible – the step-by-step method is in [the demand-testing path](/en/journeys/test-demand).

## A worked example: Tanvir's pickles

Say Tanvir in Bogura wants to sell his family-recipe mango pickle to buyers in Dhaka. His one page looks like this:

```text
One-page startup plan – Tanvir's Pickles (hypothetical example) – July 2026

1. Problem: busy people in Dhaka can't get honest, home-style pickle;
   they don't trust the store-bought jars.
2. First customers: people from Bogura and Rajshahi living in Dhaka;
   members of Facebook cooking groups.
3. Current workaround: they have relatives carry jars from home, or
   settle for a store brand.
4. Solution: homemade pickle made after the order, couriered nationwide.
5. Why switch: family recipe, made fresh per order, the making date
   written on every jar.
6. Channel: Facebook page, posts in cooking groups, word of mouth.
7. Price: 400 taka for a 400-gram jar; courier charge on the customer.
8. Margin per sale: 400 − 220 (mango, oil, spices, jar, label)
   = 180 taka.
9. Monthly costs: boosting 6,000 + packaging, photos and sundries 3,000
   = 9,000 taka.
10. Break-even: 9,000 ÷ 180 = 50 jars a month.
11. Riskiest assumption: whether people will buy pickle from an unknown
    page without tasting it.
    Test: launch a 100-taka trial pack and see if 30 sell in the first
    month, and how many buyers reorder.
12. Three tasks for 30 days: talk to 20 people in the cooking groups,
    make a 30-pack trial batch, open the page and post pre-orders.
```

Notice that the arithmetic in box 10 handed Tanvir his monthly target: no profit before 50 jars, so the first question becomes "where will 50 orders a month come from." And box 11 decided where the first money goes – not into boosting, but into trial packs.

## Keep the plan alive

- First draft in half an hour; polishing comes later. Leaving a box empty is allowed – write "don't know, need to find out."
- A 15-minute review on the first of each month: which box got proven wrong? Change it, and change the date too. Changing the plan isn't losing – it's proof you're learning.
- The page is a list of assumptions, not a list of promises. Every box is really a question: "is this true?" Running the business means proving the answers one box at a time.
- If you ever need to show someone, this is enough. When an investor or a bank wants a bigger document, build it then – from this one page.

Rule of thumb: **a plan that doesn't fit on one page doesn't fit in your head either.**
