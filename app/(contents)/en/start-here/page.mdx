# Start Here: Building a Startup or Small Business in Bangladesh

> **TL;DR:** Starting a business does not mean incorporating on day one. First identify whose problem you are solving, whether they will commit money or time, and what the smallest useful test can teach. Registration, payments, accounts, team and funding decisions come afterward, in stages.

This page is for someone with an idea but no clear first move. The useful question is: **which action will teach the most while putting the least at risk?**

If the goal is already clear, such as registering a company, accepting payments or raising funding, use the [Journeys](/en/journeys) section for an ordered path.

## 1. Identify What You Are Building

Not every business is a startup, and a startup is not necessarily an app. A startup searches for a repeatable model that could reach a large market. An SME may use a familiar model and focus from the beginning on profit, cash and reliable operations.

| Type | Common shape | First objective |
|---|---|---|
| Startup | Unproven model, rapid learning, a larger market | Find a model that works repeatedly |
| SME | Familiar product or service, defined market | Keep profit, cash and operations healthy |
| Agency or service | Client projects or retainers | Turn expertise into reliable revenue |
| E-commerce or commerce | Sourcing, sales, delivery, returns and support | Control margin, orders and fulfilment |

**First task:** Read [Startup vs SME vs Agency vs E-commerce](/en/start-here/startup-vs-sme), then write what you are building and what you want to prove in the next thirty days.

## 2. Clarify the Problem Before the Solution

“Build an app” is not a customer problem. Write:

```text
We help [specific people] with [a painful problem],
which they currently handle through [the present alternative].
```

A useful problem recurs, already costs time, money or stress, and creates a meaningful consequence when left unsolved.

## 3. Interview Potential Customers

Use twenty to thirty conversations as a starting target, not a law. Ask about actual past behaviour rather than future promises.

| Ask | Avoid |
|---|---|
| “When did this last happen?” | “Do you like my idea?” |
| “What do you do now?” | “Would you buy it?” |
| “What does it cost?” | “Do you need this feature?” |
| “What have you tried?” | “Would everyone want this?” |
| “Who decides?” | “Will you use it after launch?” |

Record the person, problem, current method, cost, decision-maker and next follow-up.

## 4. Run the Smallest Useful Test

An MVP is the smallest test of the riskiest assumption, not an unfinished version of the whole product.

| Idea | First test |
|---|---|
| Local food delivery | Take and fulfil twenty orders through a page and sheet |
| Shop-accounting software | Prepare weekly reports manually for five shops |
| Online course | Run one live batch before building a platform |
| B2B supply | Manually match ten buyers with five suppliers |
| Service booking | Use one landing page, messages and phone confirmation |

Look for use, payment, an advance, a written pilot commitment or repeat behaviour. Likes and polite praise are not traction.

## 5. Ask for Money or a Strong Commitment

For some products, payment will not arrive in the first week. Still ask for a concrete next step: a scheduled demo, shared data, a dated pilot, written approval or the start of an internal purchasing process.

Treat “tell me when it launches” as interest, not proof.

## 6. Handle Legal and Tax Work by Stage

| Stage | What to consider |
|---|---|
| Research | Interviews, problem notes and consent where relevant |
| Small test | Payment, refund, liability and data |
| Regular sales | Trade license, TIN, banking, payments and accounts |
| Co-founders or team | Written roles, ownership, IP and duties |
| Funding or corporate buyers | Company structure, clean accounts and contracts |
| Regulated sector | Sector permits and professional advice |

Check official sources before a legal, tax or VAT decision. Rules can change.

## 7. Design Payment, Delivery and Accounts Early

For consumer sales, decide how to take payment, issue a receipt, handle a failed delivery and process a refund. For B2B sales, understand the purchase order, invoice, payment term and approval chain. The user, decision-maker and person releasing payment may be different people.

## 8. A Practical First Thirty Days

| Days | Work | Output |
|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | Write the problem, customer and assumptions | One-page idea |
| 4–10 | Interview potential customers | Notes and recurring patterns |
| 11–14 | Map current alternatives | Competitor and alternative map |
| 15–20 | Run a small test | Page, sheet, demo or pilot |
| 21–25 | Ask for payment or commitment | Order, advance, pilot or written interest |
| 26–28 | Record the numbers | Cost, price, margin and conversion |
| 29–30 | Decide | Continue, change, stop or formalise |
