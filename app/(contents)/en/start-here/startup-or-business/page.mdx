# Should You Build a Startup or a Conventional Business?

> **In short:** A startup is not “big” while a conventional business is “small.” One is still searching for a repeatable model, while the other can build income around familiar demand. Before choosing, write down essential monthly expenses, available cash, family obligations, and whether you want to share ownership. Test small before leaving a job. Rule of thumb: **small test first, large bet later.**

Suppose Rahim wants to open a Facebook clothing store in Chattogram. Sumiya wants to build bookkeeping software for Dhaka's small shops. Rahim first needs to know whether monthly sales leave a profit. Sumiya first needs to know whether many shops will use the same software.

Neither person is less ambitious. The uncertainty, money, and growth path are simply different. The question is which path fits your current life.

---

## Examine the Shape of the Business First

[What is a startup?](/en/start-here/what-is-a-startup) explains the concept in depth. For this decision, compare the practical differences:

| Question | Business with a familiar model | Startup |
|---|---|---|
| How well demand is known | People already buy the product or service | The customer and workable model are still being tested |
| How it grows | Adds people, branches, equipment, or inventory | Takes the same product or system to many customers |
| First financial priority | Organise sales, cash, and profit quickly | Prove the model before accelerating growth |
| Ownership | May remain with one person or known partners | Outside investors may receive ownership |
| Main risk | Margin, sales, debt, and daily operations | No demand, a failed model, or running out of money |

The two columns can overlap. A startup may operate on customer revenue from the beginning. A conventional business can use modern technology and become very large. Examine the economics, not the status of the label.

---

## Which Path Does the Idea Need?

Write down two answers:

1. Do many people or businesses share the problem, or is demand limited to a location and a small customer group?
2. If customers grow tenfold, will people, branches, and costs also grow almost tenfold?

If the same solution can reach many customers, the idea may have a startup shape. If every new customer requires nearly the same extra people and premises, plan around conventional-business economics.

“Nationwide” is not a requirement. A small profitable market may support an excellent business. A large market alone does not create a startup either. You still have to prove that customers will pay.

---

## Calculate Your Personal Runway

Runway is the number of months your available cash can cover you without new business income. The calculation is not just the bank balance. Subtract reliable side income from essential monthly expenses.

Suppose Sumiya's household needs BDT 40,000 a month. Reliable freelance work brings in BDT 15,000, leaving BDT 25,000 to come from savings. With BDT 500,000 in the bank, her runway is:

```text
BDT 500,000 ÷ BDT 25,000 = 20 months
```

Those 20 months are not a safety guarantee. Sumiya must compare them with the time required to reach her next major proof. If a six-month test can find the first 20 paying shops, the plan looks one way. If product development alone requires two years, it looks very different.

Write down three numbers:

- essential personal and household spending
- reliable income that does not depend on the startup
- cash remaining after any immediate business contribution

Add upcoming debt payments, medical costs, or education expenses. Do not count hoped-for funding as reliable income.

---

## Household Risk Is Part of the Business Calculation

Does anyone else depend on your income? Are medical costs, school fees, rent, or debt payments likely to rise in the next year? These are not concerns outside the business model. They determine how much risk you can carry.

Do not simply announce “I am starting a business” to the family. Sit down with four numbers: monthly spending, cash savings, side income, and the month when you will review the decision. If people disagree, write down why. Fear about money, time, and uncertainty are different problems.

Check whether you can test before leaving a job. Talk to customers at night or on weekends. Run a small manual service or take advance orders. You can learn a great deal before accepting the larger risk.

---

## Can You Share Ownership and Control?

Equity means ownership in a company. When an investor provides money for equity, that investor becomes an owner as well. Depending on the agreement, some major decisions may require investor consent.

Ask yourself:

- Are you willing to share company accounts regularly?
- Are you willing to record major decisions?
- Can you accept a smaller percentage if the entire company becomes more valuable?
- Do you want the pressure to grow quickly?

Not every startup must raise venture capital. Customer revenue, founder money, grants, and debt can also support the business. But taking an equity investor requires a genuine willingness to share ownership.

---

## The Wrong Path Produces the Wrong First Job

Suppose Tanvir sells homemade pickles in Bogura. Every order needs ingredients, bottles, packing, and courier service. He spends months approaching investors under the label “food-tech startup.” His real first jobs were fixing margin and bringing old customers back.

In the opposite case, Nasrin can offer the same online class from her Sylhet coaching centre to students in other districts. She still runs every new batch like a separate offline branch and never tests whether the content can be reused. She may miss the scalable opportunity.

The problem in both stories is not the name itself. The damage comes from making the wrong task the first task.

## Write Your Answers to Five Questions

| Question | What the answer reveals |
|---|---|
| Can the same solution serve many customers? | The idea's growth path |
| What is the next major proof, and how long may it take? | The runway you need |
| Whose life is affected if income falls? | Your personal risk limit |
| Are you willing to share ownership and decisions? | Whether equity funding may fit |
| What can you test beside your current income? | Whether a large risk is necessary now |

No single answer decides the path. Put all five together. Does the idea have a startup shape while your runway is short? The answer need not be “never.” Increase side income, cut spending, or make the test smaller first.
