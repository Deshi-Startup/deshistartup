# Startup Glossary: Bangla-English Startup Terms

> **In short:** The startup world uses a great deal of English jargon, and an unknown term can lock you out of a decision. This page explains 50 useful terms in plain language. Do not memorise it. Use find-in-page when needed. If a term remains unclear in a meeting, stop and ask. That is safer than nodding through a condition you do not understand.

Imagine Sumaiya at her first investor meeting for her software startup. The questions come fast: "What's your MRR? Your CAC-to-LTV ratio? How's churn?" She built the product with her own hands and knows her customers by name – but she can't parse the questions. The meeting ends, and the late-night googling begins. This page is that night's reading, collected in one place.

And when you hear an unfamiliar term in a meeting, ask. It costs you nothing. Nodding along without understanding is the real danger, because the conditions hide inside the terms.

## From idea to product

- **Startup** – a new business whose model isn't proven yet, but which can grow very fast once it works. The full explanation is in [What is a startup?](../start-here/what-is-a-startup)
- **MVP (minimum viable product)** – the smallest product or experiment that can test your riskiest assumption. Not a half-built bad product; a small but working test.
- **Prototype** – the first sample, built to show and to learn. Not for selling; for understanding.
- **Beta** – a trial version released to a small group of customers, released precisely so the flaws surface.
- **Early adopter** – the customer who grabs new things first. They tolerate bugs, give feedback, and are the best teachers of the early days.
- **Pivot** – changing a major part of the business after learning from a test. The customer, problem, solution, price, or sales route may change.
- **Product-market fit (PMF)** – strong demand for a product within a defined market. Customers continue using it, buy again, or recommend it. No single metric proves the condition.

## Customers and growth

- **B2B / B2C** – selling to businesses versus selling to consumers. Supplying soap to shops is B2B; selling to buyers from your page is B2C.
- **Lead** – a potential customer who has shown interest. The person writing "dam koto?" in your inbox isn't a buyer yet – they're a lead.
- **Conversion** – an interested person becoming a buyer. Measured as a rate: 100 people ask the price and 8 buy, that's 8 percent conversion.
- **Funnel** – the picture of the steps from seeing to buying, with people dropping off at each step. Like a funnel: many pour in at the top, a few come out the bottom.
- **Traction** – behavioural or numerical evidence that the business is moving. Orders, revenue, regular usage, and returning customers are examples.
- **Retention** – the ability to keep customers. Of the people who bought this month, the share who come back next month.
- **Churn** – retention's flip side: the rate at which customers leave. A leaky bucket – pour in as much as you like; if the hole is big, it never fills.
- **Referral** – new customers arriving through old ones, spontaneously or for a reward.
- **Organic vs paid** – customers who arrive without ad spend (search, shares, word of mouth) versus customers who came from ads.
- **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** – the average cost of winning one new customer. Spend 10,000 taka on boosting and get 20 new orders: CAC is 500 taka.
- **LTV (lifetime value)** – the total gross profit a customer contributes over the relationship. Comparing CAC with revenue alone can hide the cost of serving that customer.

## The money maths

- **Revenue** – the total money that came in from sales, before any costs are deducted. Earning revenue and making a profit are not the same thing.
- **Gross margin** – the percentage of sales left after direct cost. On a BDT 400 jar with BDT 220 of ingredients, gross profit is BDT 180 and gross margin is 45 percent.
- **Burn rate** – how much more you spend than you earn, per month. Earning 1 lakh and spending 3 lakh means a burn of 2 lakh taka a month.
- **Runway** – how many months you can survive on the money at hand: cash divided by burn. 10 lakh in the bank at 2 lakh monthly burn is a 5-month runway.
- **Break-even** – the sales level at which the month's costs are fully covered; profit starts beyond it. The method is in [the one-page plan](../start-here/one-page-startup-plan).
- **Cash flow** – the stream of money in and out. You can be profitable on paper and still have no cash in hand if you sell on credit – that's exactly when businesses choke.
- **MRR / ARR (monthly / annual recurring revenue)** – income that arrives on schedule every month or year in subscription-style businesses. One-off sales don't count.
- **Unit economics** – the profit-and-loss maths of a single unit: what's left on one order or one customer. Lose money on one unit and a thousand units lose a thousand times as much.
- **Bootstrapping** – growing without outside investors, on your own savings and the business's own income.

## Funding and investors

For the whole system behind these terms, read the [funding roadmap](../funding-roadmap).

- **Angel investor** – an individual investing small amounts of their own money at the earliest stage. Often brings advice and introductions along with the cheque.
- **VC (venture capital)** – a firm that pools other people's money into a fund and invests it in startups. It takes risk deliberately; a few big wins pay for the whole fund.
- **Pre-seed / seed / Series A** – the stages of funding rounds. Seed means exactly that: money to prove the idea. Series A comes after evidence the model works, to scale it.
- **Pitch and pitch deck** – a pitch is the structured explanation you give an investor. A pitch deck is the short slide document shown with it. Length varies by meeting and investor.
- **Incubator** – a programme that may support an early team with learning, mentors, workspace, or a network. Benefits and timelines vary.
- **Accelerator** – a time-bound programme intended to help an operating team move faster. Some provide money, some take equity, and some offer training only. Read the terms before applying.
- **Demo day** – the final day of a programme, when every team pitches in front of investors.
- **Grant** – money that normally requires neither loan repayment nor equity. It can still carry conditions on work, spending, and reporting.
- **Term sheet** – the draft of an investment's key terms: how much money, at what valuation, with what rights. Signing it isn't the final contract, but it sets the direction.
- **Due diligence** – the investor's close inspection before the money moves: your books, your paperwork, every claim you've made. Like verifying the deed before buying land.
- **Convertible note / SAFE** – an agreement that provides money now and issues shares later under defined rules. The trigger, price, and conditions vary by document. Do not sign without legal advice.
- **Exit** – the event where investors or founders turn their stake into cash, usually a company sale or a stock-market listing.

## Equity and company paperwork

- **Equity** – ownership share in the company. It's what investors get in exchange for their money.
- **Share** – the unit that ownership is measured in. However many shares you hold, that's your stake.
- **Valuation** – the agreed price of the whole company. There are before-the-money and after-the-money versions – check which one your term sheet uses.
- **Dilution** – existing owners' percentages shrinking when new shares are issued. Your slice gets smaller, but if the company grows, a smaller slice can be worth more.
- **Cap table** – the complete list of who holds how many shares. It's among the first things an investor asks to see.
- **Vesting** – earning shares gradually after meeting time or work conditions. The period and conditions belong in the agreement.
- **ESOP (employee stock option pool)** – equity reserved to give employees a future option to receive shares. An employee may receive nothing if the stated conditions are not met.
- **MoA / AoA** – the company's two founding documents: what the company will do (MoA) and how it will run internally (AoA). Filed with RJSC at registration; details in [Company types](../company-types).
- **Authorized / paid-up capital** – authorized capital is the maximum value of shares the company may issue under its documents. Paid-up capital is the amount shareholders have actually paid for issued shares.
- **IP (intellectual property)** – the brand name, the logo, the software's code, your own recipe. Investors check whose name it's registered under during due diligence.
- **Compliance** – the recurring work of staying legal: filing returns, VAT records, licence renewals. Not a one-time task – an annual routine.
