# A 30-Day Roadmap for Starting a Startup in Bangladesh

> **In short:** There is no shortcut to a successful startup in 30 days, but there is no reason to hide with an idea for a month. Interview 10 to 12 people in week one. Make a small priced offer in week two. Ask for real commitment in week three. Deliver the solution manually to one or two people in the final week. At the end, use behaviour rather than compliments to decide whether to continue, change one thing, or stop.

The goal is not to produce an app, logo, or company. It is to answer four questions:

1. Does the problem happen repeatedly?
2. Who feels it most strongly?
3. Will that person give up something meaningful, such as money, time, or data, for a solution?
4. Can you deliver a small version of the solution?

Thirty days is a work constraint, not a promise of results. B2B sales, government approval, or complex technology may take longer. Extend the calendar when needed, but keep the evidence sequence.

---

## What Each Week Should Produce

| Week | Work | Evidence at the end |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Understand the problem and people | Notes from 10 to 12 conversations, with recurring problems marked |
| 2 | Make a small priced offer | A one-page offer and manually delivered example |
| 3 | Ask for commitment | Money, time, data, or another concrete next step |
| 4 | Deliver small and measure | Usage, outcome, cost, and the next decision |

“Evidence” does not mean praise. Saying “good idea” is easy. Paying a deposit, joining a pilot, or providing data for a test is harder. The harder action is usually a better sign of interest.

---

## Week 1: Forget the Solution and Hear the Problem

Do not build a product during the first seven days. Find people who genuinely encounter the problem. Begin with introductions if needed, but do not interview only friends trying to encourage you.

### Days 1 and 2: Write One Line About the Person and Problem

```text
[Which person] faces [which difficulty] while trying to [which job].
```

“Small businesses have bad accounts” is too broad. “Facebook clothing sellers in Chattogram cannot reconcile courier settlements and returned orders in one ledger” can be tested.

### Days 3 to 6: Interview 10 to 12 People

Ask:

- When did this problem last occur?
- What did you do then?
- How much time or money did it cost?
- Have you tried another solution?
- Who suffers if the work fails?

Do not lead with your idea and distort the answer. What people did last time is more useful than what they predict they will do later.

### Day 7: Mark the Stories That Repeat

Look for the same problem in several accounts. There is no magic threshold. If three of ten people describe a recent, painful version of the same problem, there is a reason to investigate further. If all ten describe different problems, the customer group may still be too broad.

---

## Week 2: Make a Priced Offer, Not a Complete Product

An MVP is the smallest test that can examine the riskiest assumption. It is not half of an unfinished app. A phone call, Google Sheet, Facebook page, or manual service may be enough.

### Days 8 to 10: Choose the Riskiest Assumption

Which error would stop the entire idea?

- people do not consider the problem urgent
- they will not pay for the solution
- you cannot deliver the promised result
- you cannot reach the right customer

Test one assumption at a time. If you combine four, you will not know why the result appeared.

### Days 11 to 13: Build a Manual Version

Suppose Sumiya is considering bookkeeping software. Before writing code, she can organise sales for three shops in Google Sheets and send a weekly report. She will learn whether owners use the report before building the product.

### Day 14: State a Price

Do not ask, “How much would you pay?” Set a price using your cost and the customer benefit. Then ask, “Would you try this next week at this price?” Negotiation, rejection, and questions are all useful evidence.

---

## Week 3: Ask for Commitment, Not Praise

Return to the people you interviewed and show the offer to several new prospects. Aim for ten honest sales conversations, not ten encouraging comments.

Commitment can look different by business:

- a refundable deposit or preorder for a consumer product
- a small paid B2B pilot
- another meeting, required data, or decision-maker time for a complex sale
- an agreed date for a usage test

Not all commitments are equal. An emoji or “tell me when you launch” is a weak sign. Giving time, information, or money is stronger.

If you accept advance money, write down what you will deliver, when you will deliver it, and how refunds work. A validation test that destroys trust damages the real business.

---

## Week 4: Deliver a Result to One or Two Customers

### Days 22 to 26: Run a Small Pilot

Do not take many customers at once. Try to produce the promised result for one or two. Record the hours required, direct costs, and every point where delivery becomes stuck.

### Days 27 to 29: Observe Behaviour

Did the customer finish the task? Did they want to use it again? Did they introduce someone else? What complaints appeared after payment? If they did not use it, why not?

“How did you like it?” attracts a polite answer. “Which day did you use it?” and “Will you pay for it next month?” reveal behaviour.

### Day 30: Choose One of Three Decisions

- **Continue:** the same problem appeared repeatedly, people made real commitments, and the small solution produced some result.
- **Change one thing:** the problem is real, but the price, customer segment, or offer did not fit. Change one variable and test again.
- **Stop:** the problem is weak and nobody will contribute time or money. Record the lesson and investigate another problem.

One sale is not final proof of demand. Ten rejections do not always kill an idea either. Examine who declined, why they declined, and what they actually did.

---

## When Legal Work Moves Forward

Customer interviews and demonstrations do not require a company on day one. Once you regularly accept money, supply products, or hire people, organise the relevant business documents. A pilot in regulated areas such as food, health, fintech, education, or imports may need approval earlier.

Use the [legal roadmap](/en/legal-roadmap) for your situation. Read [Trade alone, take partners, or form a company?](/en/company-types) before choosing the structure. Do not guess before accepting money.

## 30-Day Checklist

- [ ] I have described the person and problem in one line
- [ ] I have heard 10 to 12 recent problem stories
- [ ] I have marked the problem that repeats
- [ ] I have selected one risky assumption
- [ ] I have made a manually delivered, priced offer
- [ ] I have held ten honest sales conversations
- [ ] I have separated concrete commitment from polite praise
- [ ] I have delivered to one or two customers and recorded time and cost
- [ ] I have decided to continue, change one thing, or stop

Rule of thumb: **count evidence, not days. The purpose of 30 days is to complete one learning cycle.**
