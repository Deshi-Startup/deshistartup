# Should You Build a Startup or a Regular Business?

> **TL;DR:** The question is not "which one is better." They are two different games with different rules. A regular business wins a little every month. A startup absorbs losses for years, then wins big once, or loses everything. Which one is yours depends on three things: how big your idea is *forced* to become, how many months you can survive without income, and whether you could absorb a total loss. Write honest answers to this guide's five questions today, on one page. That alone gets you halfway to the decision.

Imagine two people quitting their jobs in the same month. Rahim opens a clothing page on Facebook in Chattogram with 3 lakh taka of savings. Sumaiya teams up with two co-founders in Dhaka to build an app that keeps the books for small shops.

Fast-forward three years. Rahim's page sells around 3 lakh taka a month and clears about 60 thousand in profit, and the business is entirely his. Sumaiya's company is still losing money on paper, yet seven thousand shops run on her app, the team is fifteen people, and investors value the company at several crore taka. Both succeeded. Their lives look nothing alike.

Which life is yours? That is the only question this guide exists to answer.

## Two paths, two different bets

What exactly counts as a startup, and which business belongs in which box, is covered in [What is a startup?](/en/start-here/what-is-a-startup) and [Startup vs SME vs agency vs e-commerce](/en/start-here/startup-vs-sme-vs-agency-vs-e-commerce-business-in-bangladesh). Here, let's just lay the terms of each bet side by side:

| | Regular business | Startup |
|---|---|---|
| Where money comes from | Own savings, family loans, bank | Your own at first, then investors in exchange for equity |
| When profit arrives | Target it within months | Years later; growth comes first |
| Ownership | Entirely yours | Divided as investors come in |
| If you win | Solid monthly income, full independence | A company worth crores |
| If you lose | Losses are limited; you can wind down and walk away | Years of time and money can go to zero |

The math is simple. A regular business gives you a high chance of a medium win. A startup gives you a low chance of a huge win — most startups don't survive. Neither path is beneath the other. The bets are just different.

## Ask your idea first

This is not purely a question of preference. Ideas have a shape, and the shape largely picks the path for you.

Some ideas cannot stay small. Ride sharing doesn't work in one neighbourhood — you need drivers and riders by the thousands, or nobody shows up. An idea like that either grows big or dies. Other ideas cannot grow big. The best restaurant in your area doesn't spread across the country overnight — every new city means new cooks, new rent, new headaches.

So put two questions to the idea. One: is this a problem for the whole country, or for my area? Two: if customers grow tenfold, do costs grow nearly tenfold too, or only a little? The deeper three-question test is in [the earlier guide](/en/start-here/what-is-a-startup).

## Then ask your life

An idea shaped like a startup is not, by itself, permission to jump. You also need the capacity to carry the bet. That gets measured in three places — your pocket, your family, and your appetite for risk.

**Pocket: measure your runway.** Runway is the number of months your savings can run the household with no income coming in. It's the same mental math you do when the salary runs out before the month does — how long will the savings last — just written down properly. Say Sumaiya's household costs 40 thousand taka a month and she has 5 lakh in the bank. Her runway is twelve and a half months. But getting a product built and reaching the first funding realistically takes 18 months or more. That's a six-month gap — cut costs, save more, or line up a side income before starting. Founders who skip this calculation lose the bet before they even sit at the table.

**Family: have the honest conversation.** Are you the only earner in the household? Who covers your parents' medicine and your siblings' school fees for two years? There is no shame in the question — it's reality. Without the family on board, carrying two years of losses is nearly impossible.

**Risk: picture the zero.** Three years of work, nothing in hand — hold that picture in your mind. Could you take it? At twenty-five, there's time to recover. With a household and loan instalments on your shoulders, the math changes.

Ownership belongs here too. Taking investor money means taking on partners — monthly reporting, sign-offs on big decisions. If "answering to no one" is the reason you're leaving your job, the startup path puts you right back in a cage.

## The money doors are different too

The money path for a regular business is familiar — your own savings, family loans, and once sales are steady, an SME loan from a bank. Banks look for running sales and collateral, and an established business has both.

For a startup, that bank door is mostly shut — no profit, no collateral. The main path is equity: giving up a share of the company's ownership in exchange for investor money.

A government door has opened as well. Under Bangladesh Bank's circular of July 2025, startup loans are capped at 4 percent interest, limits run from 2 to 8 crore taka depending on the age of the business, and banks may now also invest in startup equity. Read the conditions, though — you need technology or a new model, demonstrable potential to scale fast, and a clean record with no loan defaults. What this means for you: the "startup" label is worth real money even in the government's books, but the door only opens for models that genuinely scale. Forcing the label opens no doors at all.

## The five-question scorecard

Now bring it all together. Take pen and paper and write an honest answer next to each question:

| # | Question | "Yes" points to | "No" points to |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Can technology spread this solution across the whole country? | Startup | Regular business |
| 2 | If customers grow tenfold, do costs grow far less? | Startup | Regular business |
| 3 | Can I survive at least 18 months without income? | Startup | Regular business |
| 4 | Could I recover if I lost everything? | Startup | Regular business |
| 5 | Am I willing to share ownership and decisions with investors? | Startup | Regular business |

How to read it: the first two questions are about the idea, the last three about your life. Five yeses means you have the ground to attempt a startup. If the idea gets two noes, don't force it — build an excellent regular business and keep the profit from day one. Idea says yes but life says no somewhere? Then the answer isn't "no," it's "not yet" — close the gap first. Start small alongside a job, save for a year, find a co-founder. There are ways.

## The cost of the wrong costume

The mistake happens in both directions, and both are expensive.

Imagine Tanvir in Bogura dressing his homemade pickle business up as a "food-tech startup" in a pitch deck. He spends a year chasing investors — trips to Dhaka, demo days, email after email. No money comes, because the model doesn't actually scale, and investors see that in five minutes. A year spent on his existing customers instead would have put the business on its feet.

Now picture the reverse. Nasrin in Sylhet has moved from her coaching centre to an online course platform — an idea that genuinely scales. But she refuses investment and grows slowly on her own savings. Meanwhile a funded competitor with the same idea takes the national market in two years. In a game where growing first is how you survive, going slow isn't the safe option — it's the riskiest one.

## The decision isn't forever

Two things take the pressure off.

First, there is a middle path. Many Bangladeshi software companies started as agencies — client work pays the bills while the product takes shape on the side. Income keeps flowing and the risk gets split. The [four-types guide](/en/start-here/startup-vs-sme-vs-agency-vs-e-commerce-business-in-bangladesh) covers this route in detail.

Second, whichever path you pick, the first step is the same — test small. Startup or shop, first spend a little money finding out whether people actually buy. The full method lives in the [idea validation](/en/idea-validation) section. Six months from now, you'll be deciding with far more knowledge than you have today.

So the rule of thumb fits in one line: **build a startup only when the idea cannot stay small and your life can carry a big bet.** Everything else in this guide is a footnote to that line.
