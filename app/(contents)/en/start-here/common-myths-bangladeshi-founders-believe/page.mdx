# Common Myths Bangladeshi Founders Believe

> **TL;DR:** A new founder's most precious assets are time and savings, and myths eat both. This page breaks eight familiar myths, each with the real picture and one thing to do about it. If one of them matches something you currently believe, do its action today – the rest of the guide can wait.

Myths spread in warm, well-meaning voices. An uncle says, "don't tell anyone the idea." A Facebook group comment says, "get an investor first, brother." A motivational YouTube video teaches that raising funding means you've won. The people saying these things mean well, which is exactly why the words stick. The problem is that most of them are wrong, and the price is paid in months of wandering and burnt savings.

Let's break them one by one.

## About ideas and starting

### 1. "I can't tell anyone the idea, it'll get stolen"

It sounds cautious, so it feels believable. In practice, describing an idea is easy and building the business is hard. People rarely commit months of work merely after hearing a sentence. Secrecy creates another problem: without conversations, nothing gets validated. Keep code, contracts, and genuinely proprietary details private when needed. You can still discuss the customer's problem without revealing them.

**Do this:** Tell at least ten potential customers about the idea this week and note down their questions. The full method is in the [idea validation](../idea-validation) section.

### 2. "I'll finish the product completely, then go to market"

It feels like showing something unfinished will cost you respect. The truth runs the other way – until you're in the market, you don't even know what "finished" means. The feature you spent six months building may be one your customers never needed. Launch with an MVP (the smallest working version) and let customer feedback shape the rest.

**Do this:** Define a version of the idea small enough to put in real customers' hands within one month.

### 3. "It worked abroad, so a straight copy will work here"

You can import a model; you can't import the market's reality. Customers here are used to cash on delivery, hesitate to pay strangers in advance, and delivery outside Dhaka is still a headache. A foreign model built on online payments and next-day delivery will jam in all three places if copied as-is. The companies that have made it here took the model apart and rebuilt it around local reality.

**Do this:** Next to your favourite foreign model, write down where Bangladesh's realities (payments, delivery, trust) will break it, and what you'll change.

## About funding

### 4. "First I'll raise funding, then I'll start the business"

This is an expensive myth because it can leave people waiting instead of starting. Large funding headlines can also distort the picture. [LightCastle's 2025 review](https://lightcastlepartners.com/insights/2026/01/startup-investments-report-2025-yir/) counted USD 124 million across 12 disclosed deals. One strategic transaction, the ShopUp–Sary merger, accounted for USD 110 million. The headline total therefore says little about access to early-stage capital. Build evidence of a painful problem and willingness to pay first.

**Do this:** Write a one-page plan for making your first ten sales without any funding. That plan later becomes your strongest proof in front of investors.

### 5. "Getting funded means we've made it"

Funding headlines look like a finish line. Funding is fuel, not a destination. Equity funding divides ownership. Depending on the agreement, it can also bring reporting, board consent, and growth targets. Whether the money helps must be judged through customers, revenue, and costs.

**Do this:** Pick your own success numbers – how many customers return, what the margin per order is. Whether someone raised funding is other people's news.

## About customers and the market

### 6. "I have no competitors"

It sounds good in a pitch; to an investor's ear it's an alarm bell. No competitors usually means one of two things – either there's no market, or you haven't looked in the right places. Remember that your real competitor is often not an app. Paper ledgers, trust in the familiar shopkeeper, and "things are fine as they are" – those three are the biggest competitors in Bangladesh.

**Do this:** Ask ten potential customers how they handle the problem today. Every answer is a competitor – make the list.

### 7. "Everyone is my customer"

The bigger the market sounds, the better – that's how "all 170 million people are my market" gets said. In practice, aiming at everyone means reaching no one: the ad budget spreads thin and the message lands on nobody. Say Sumaiya tries to sell her bookkeeping app as "for every business" – and gets silence. Then she targets only clothing-page sellers and writes in their language, "match your stock and courier payments in one place" – and now they respond. To a small group you're essential; to everyone you're just another ad.

**Do this:** Write one line describing exactly who your first 100 customers are – trade, city, problem. If it doesn't fit in one line, the group is still too big.

### 8. "Sales jump on discounts, so we've found product-market fit"

First, the term. Product-market fit means a product has developed strong demand in a specific market. Customers continue using it, buy again, or recommend it. No single metric proves the condition. One hundred orders at a 30 percent discount show interest, but they do not prove the whole product. Track what happens when those customers face the normal price.

**Do this:** Keep discount customers in a separate group. Measure how many buy again at the normal price next month. Do not change advertising, price, and product simultaneously, or you will not know what changed the result.

## The real point is one line

All eight myths have the same cure: replace belief with proof. It doesn't matter how sweet the advice sounded – did the customer pay, did they come back, that's the verdict. Make it your rule of thumb: **proof first, belief after.**
