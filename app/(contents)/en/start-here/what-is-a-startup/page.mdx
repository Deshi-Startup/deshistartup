# What Is a Startup?

> **In short:** A new shop, Facebook page, or app is not automatically a startup. A startup is still searching for a business model while trying to take the same solution to many customers quickly. If ten times as many customers require ten times as many people, branches, and costs, the model does not yet scale. Write three lines today: whose problem you solve, how you could reach many people, and which major assumption remains untested.

Suppose two people start businesses on the same day. Rahim opens a restaurant in Chattogram. Sumiya is building bookkeeping software for small shops. Both are entrepreneurs, but their growth equations differ.

To enter another neighbourhood, Rahim needs another site, kitchen, and team. Sumiya can offer the same software to shops in another city. Her server and support costs will rise, but she does not rebuild the entire product for every customer. That difference is the beginning of understanding a startup.

---

## A Startup Is a Search

At the beginning, a founder usually does not know three answers:

- which customer feels the problem most strongly
- which solution that customer will pay for
- whether the same method can repeatedly bring in new customers

A startup therefore begins as a search, not a certain business. Founders talk to customers, build a small product, test prices, and change course after seeing the results. That is how they discover a workable model.

One sale is not enough. The method must work repeatedly. A friend buys today, a stranger arrives through an advert tomorrow, and someone in another city buys for the same reason next month. Now the business is no longer resting only on personal connections.

---

## Scale Means Customers Grow Faster Than Cost

Scale means serving more customers without costs rising at the same rate. Cost will not disappear. The question is whether each new customer requires almost the same work from the beginning.

Suppose 50 shops use Sumiya's software and each pays BDT 1,000 a month. Monthly revenue is BDT 50,000, while servers and support cost BDT 35,000.

Now suppose 500 shops use it. Revenue becomes BDT 500,000 while servers and support rise to BDT 150,000. Customers have grown tenfold, but those two costs have not. The figures are illustrative, but they make the mechanism visible.

This does not make a restaurant a bad business. A restaurant needs new places and people as it expands. Software can give the same product to many customers. The goals and economics are different.

---

## Technology Alone Does Not Make a Startup

Imagine building an app for a shop while every order still requires the same amount of manual work. The app does not turn the model into a startup. A new supply method or operating model may also create a scalable path without beginning as an app.

Technology is a tool. It can reach distant customers, automate repeated work, or help a team learn faster from data. But impressive technology with no customer demand is not a business.

So the first question is not “Which app should we build?” Ask, “Whose recurring problem is painful enough that they will pay to solve it?”

---

## Keep Programme Definitions Separate from the Working Definition

When applying for a government loan or fund, follow that programme's own definition. Bangladesh Bank's [9 July 2025 master circular on startup financing](https://www.bb.org.bd/mediaroom/circulars/smespd/jul092025smespd02.pdf) describes technology-dependent, innovative ventures capable of rapid scale. Financing applicants must meet further conditions, including registration in Bangladesh. An operating enterprise more than 12 years past registration is not eligible under that facility.

That is an eligibility rule for a particular financing programme. When examining your idea, the working question is simpler: can the same model serve many customers?

Calling yourself a startup does not create government eligibility. Failing one programme's conditions does not make your business bad either. Use the definition attached to the door you are trying to enter.

---

## Ask Your Idea Four Questions

Write down the answers:

1. Do many people or businesses face the same problem?
2. Can the same solution move from one city to another?
3. If customers grow tenfold, will people, branches, and costs also grow almost tenfold?
4. Which major answer remains unknown: willingness to pay, price, or a reliable route to customers?

If the first two answers are yes, the third is no, and the fourth identifies a real uncertainty, the idea may have a startup shape. That is not proof. Run a small test.

A different result is not a failure. A profitable shop, agency, or commerce business is not a lesser ambition. Just do not enter the field using the wrong rules.
