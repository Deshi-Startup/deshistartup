# Founder Readiness Checklist

> **TL;DR:** Being prepared doesn't mean being less brave — it means arranging the shock absorbers before the shock. Test yourself in the five areas below: skills, time, money, family, mindset. Every empty tick is a task, not a failing grade. Sit down for fifteen minutes today, mark the whole list, then write a one-line plan next to every empty box.

Imagine it's two in the morning. The idea is spinning in your head and sleep won't come. A voice says: hand in the resignation letter tomorrow. This page exists for exactly that moment. The excitement is precious — it's what will carry you a long way. But before quitting your job, you owe yourself twenty calm minutes of work.

## How to use this

- Mark it alone, honestly. Nobody else will see this sheet.
- A box you can't tick yet doesn't mean "no" — it means "not yet." Write next to it when and how you'll close the gap.
- You don't have to wait for a full sheet. Which gaps you can start with, and which you can't, is covered at the end.

One more thing. This checklist measures your *readiness*. Whether your path should be a startup or a regular business is a separate question — for that, see [Should you build a startup or a regular business?](/en/start-here/should-you-build-a-startup-or-a-normal-business)

## 1. Skills: who can do the work

Every new business has three core jobs — making, selling, and keeping the books. You don't have to master all three, but you do need an answer for who covers each one.

- [ ] Wrote one line on which of the three jobs I can do and which I can't
- [ ] Arranged cover for what I can't do — a co-founder, learning it, or an early hire
- [ ] Talked to at least ten target customers
- [ ] Can do basic profit-and-loss maths myself, or have a plan to learn

Why do ten conversations belong here? Selling is a founder's number-one skill, and talking to strangers about your idea is selling in its first form. If you've done ten of those conversations, you've already passed the test.

## 2. Time: an honest weekly count

- [ ] Wrote down how many hours a week I can give — from my actual routine, not a guess
- [ ] Made myself a promise of at least 12 months of sticking with it
- [ ] If starting alongside a job, set the condition that triggers going full-time

An example makes the last box clear. Say Nasrin in Sylhet is building online courses alongside her job. Her written condition: "If course income crosses 40 thousand taka for three months in a row, I quit the job." The decision no longer depends on her mood — it stands on a number.

## 3. Money: runway and separate books

- [ ] Calculated my runway — savings divided by monthly household costs
- [ ] Budgeted the business's startup costs separately — not mixed into household money
- [ ] Built the household budget assuming zero business income for the first six months
- [ ] Set up separate business and personal money — separate account, separate records
- [ ] Not starting on borrowed money; if I am, wrote a repayment plan with dates

The runway maths fits in one line: if the household needs 35 thousand taka a month and savings are 4 lakh 20 thousand, the runway is 12 months. That number decides how big a bet you can afford — the full method is in [the decision guide](/en/start-here/should-you-build-a-startup-or-a-normal-business).

## 4. Family: the people whose lives change too

- [ ] Had the honest conversation with my spouse or parents — how long, how much money at risk, what happens if it fails
- [ ] Listed who depends on my income and secured their expenses
- [ ] Have at least one person who will stand by me in a hard stretch — if not with money, then with support

Many founders skip this conversation, thinking "I'll tell them once it's working." Do the opposite. In the bad months, your family is the last stretch of your runway — starting without telling them means leaving your biggest asset behind.

## 5. Mindset: shock absorbers

- [ ] Wrote one line on what I'd do if it fails — return to my old work, get a job, try again
- [ ] Wrote down the number: how much money I'm prepared to lose before stopping
- [ ] Have at least one proof of finishing something hard in the past two or three years
- [ ] Can hear "this idea won't work" and ask why instead of getting angry

Why draw the stopping line in advance? The most money tends to flow into a sinking business near the end — "just a little more and it'll turn around." The limit you set with a cool head is what saves you on the hot-headed days.

## The empty boxes are now your to-do list

Now count the gaps in each area. How to read them:

| Gaps in | What it means | Do this first |
|---|---|---|
| Skills | You can't do everything alone — normal | Find a co-founder, or learn it at a small scale |
| Time | No need to go full-time yet | Start alongside the job; write the full-time trigger |
| Money | The bet is still beyond your means | Save first, cut costs — never start with a money gap |
| Family | The most valuable support hasn't been secured | Have the conversation this week, with numbers |
| Mindset | The shock absorbers aren't fitted | Write the way back and the stopping line — half an hour's work |

In broad strokes: you can start with gaps in skills and time — they close along the way. Start with gaps in money and family, and the business won't sink you; you'll sink the business. So keep the rule of thumb: **the checklist comes before quitting your job, not after.**
